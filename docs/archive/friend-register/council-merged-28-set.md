### welcome · day 3 · fueling_mistake
SUBJECT: eight hours is about 24 gels  
BODY:  
{first_name} —

You signed up looking for race information. The first piece I’d send a friend is the fueling arithmetic, because the course score stops being interesting once the brain runs out of glucose.

At 75 grams of carbohydrate an hour, an eight-hour race requires 600 grams. That is roughly 24 gels if gels are doing all the work.

Your exact target will differ — body size, intensity, and gut tolerance all get a vote. The point is to do the complete arithmetic before race morning, when six gels can look like a heroic amount despite being lunch.

Practice your planned hourly intake on long rides. Use the same products and timing. If your stomach objects, adjust during training—not beside mile marker 80 while making promises to several gods.

### welcome · day 3 · sober_pacing
SUBJECT: the three parts of a long race  
BODY:  
{first_name} —

You signed up with a race in mind. A framework I wish I had used earlier is to divide a long race into three parts.

I have treated the opening 15% like the finish, then under-fueled the middle and paid for both late. That was a pacing failure, not bad luck.

The opening is restraint. The field is crowded, legs are fresh, and expensive decisions feel free.

The middle is rhythm. Hold the planned effort and eat on schedule.

The final quarter is where earlier choices become visible. If pacing and fueling held, you can still make decisions. If they did not, the course begins making them for you.

The percentages vary by event. The useful part is the sequence: restrain, settle, then race.

— Matti
