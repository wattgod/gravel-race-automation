4 · Training Zones

The Point of Zones

Training zones aren't arbitrary numbers. Each zone targets a specific physiological system. Training in the wrong zone doesn't just reduce effectiveness — it actively harms your progression by creating the wrong type of fatigue without the right type of adaptation.

The 80/20 rule: 80% of your training should be in Zones 1-2 (easy). 20% should be in Zones 4-6 (hard). Zone 3 is the "gray zone" — too hard to recover from, too easy to create real adaptation. Avoid it unless specifically prescribed.

BEFORE YOU START: FTP TEST REQUIRED

Your Week 1 priority is completing an FTP test. Without your FTP, every zone target in this plan is a percentage of an unknown number. Until you test, use RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) to guide intensity — but get this done in your first week. No structured intervals until you have your number.

After testing, recalculate all zones: Zone watts = FTP × zone percentage. If you use Zwift, TrainerRoad, or a Garmin — update your FTP setting immediately after testing.

FTP Testing Protocol

Your plan includes FTP tests at regular intervals. Here's the 20-minute test protocol:

  1. 12 minutes progressive warmup (Z1 to Z2)
  2. 5 minutes at RPE 6/10 (moderate effort)
  3. 5 minutes easy recovery (Z1)
  4. 5 minutes ALL OUT — go as hard as you can sustain
  5. 5 minutes easy recovery
  6. 20 minutes ALL OUT — start conservatively at RPE 8, adjust up. This sets your FTP.
  7. 10 minutes easy cooldown

Formula: FTP = 20-minute average power x 0.95

WARNING

Don't test when tired. Do FTP tests when fresh — not after a hard training week. The test result sets ALL your training zones for the next 6 weeks. An inaccurate test means 6 weeks of wrong-zone training.

Critical Notes on Using Zones

  • Easy means EASY. Zone 2 should feel almost too easy. If you're questioning whether you're going hard enough, you're in the right zone.
  • Hard means HARD. Zone 4+ intervals should leave you breathless. If you can hold a conversation, you're not in the right zone.
  • Don't fill the gap. The biggest mistake is spending too much time in Zone 3 (tempo). It feels productive but creates maximum fatigue with suboptimal adaptation.
  • Power > HR > RPE. Use power meter if available, heart rate as backup, RPE as last resort. Heart rate lags — use RPE for short intervals.

5 · How Adaptation Works

Every workout in this plan exists for a reason. Understanding the adaptation model helps you make smart decisions when life disrupts the plan.

The Foundational Model

STEP 1: STRESS

You apply a training stimulus (the workout). This creates controlled damage — micro-tears in muscle, glycogen depletion, cardiovascular stress.

STEP 2: RECOVERY

Your body repairs the damage. This takes 24-72 hours depending on workout intensity. Sleep, nutrition, and stress levels all affect recovery speed.

STEP 3: SUPERCOMPENSATION

Your body doesn't just repair — it overbuilds. You come back slightly stronger than before. This is the entire point of training.

STEP 4: REPEAT

Apply the next stress at the peak of supercompensation. Too soon = overtraining. Too late = detraining. The plan's timing handles this for you.

Where It Goes Wrong

THE #1 TRAINING MISTAKE

Going too hard on easy days and too easy on hard days. This puts you in the "gray zone" — maximum fatigue, minimum adaptation. Trust the zones. Easy days should feel embarrassingly easy. Hard days should be genuinely hard.

THE #2 TRAINING MISTAKE

Skipping recovery. Adaptation happens during rest, not during workouts. The workout is the stimulus. Sleep is where you get faster. Cutting sleep to fit in more training is counterproductive — you're removing the adaptation window.

Practical Rules

  • Never add intensity to an easy day. If the plan says Z2, stay in Z2. Even if you feel great.
  • Never add volume to a hard day. Hard days are about quality, not quantity. If you can do more, you didn't go hard enough.
  • If you miss a workout, skip it. Don't try to make it up by doubling the next day. The plan accounts for progressive overload — doubling creates injury risk.
  • If you're sick, stop. Training while sick extends illness duration by 2-3x. Take the days off. You'll lose less fitness from rest than from a week of half-effort sick training.
  • Sleep > training. If you have to choose between a 5am workout and 8 hours of sleep, choose sleep every time.

7 · Phase Progression

Your 20-week plan is divided into 4 phases. Each phase has a specific purpose. Don't rush through base to get to the "hard stuff" — base fitness determines your ceiling.

Base 1 — WEEKS 1-5 (Feb 14 – Mar 20)

Build aerobic foundation. All riding is Zone 1-2 with progressive volume increases. Strength is 2x/week. This phase feels easy — that's the point.

Base 2 — WEEKS 6-10 (Mar 21 – Apr 24)

Introduce low-intensity intervals (tempo, G Spot). Volume continues to build. Strength transitions to maintenance. Long rides extend by 15-20 min/week.

Build — WEEKS 11-16 (Apr 25 – Jun 5)

Race-specific intensity. VO2max and threshold intervals. Long rides include race-pace efforts. This is where it gets hard. Fatigue is expected and managed through recovery weeks.

Peak + Taper — WEEKS 17-20 (Jun 6 – Jul 3)

Sharpen fitness. Reduce volume by 30-40%, maintain intensity. Race simulation rides. Final dress rehearsal in Week 17-18. Taper begins 10-14 days before race.

RECOVERY WEEKS

Every 3rd week is a recovery week — volume drops 30-40%, intensity drops to Zone 2 only. These weeks are where adaptation happens. Skipping recovery weeks is the fastest path to overtraining and stalled progress. They are not optional.

9 · Workout Execution

UNTIL YOU COMPLETE YOUR FTP TEST

Your ZWO files use FTP-based power targets. Without your FTP number, use RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) from the zone chart instead. After your Week 1 FTP test, update your FTP in Zwift/TrainerRoad/Garmin and all workout targets will calibrate automatically.

The Execution Gap

The plan says: "4x4min at 110% FTP with 4min recovery."

What actually happens:

  • First interval: nailed it, felt strong
  • Second interval: still good, maybe went a bit hard
  • Third interval: struggling, cut 30 seconds short
  • Fourth interval: abandoned or at 95% FTP instead of 110%

Result: You did a Zone 3-4 workout instead of a Zone 5 workout. Different stimulus, different adaptation, wrong training effect.

Universal Execution Rules

RULE 1: START CONSERVATIVE

First interval of every set should feel slightly too easy. If the plan says 110% FTP, start the first rep at 105%. Build into it. The goal is completing ALL intervals at target power, not crushing the first one and failing the rest.

RULE 2: QUALITY OVER QUANTITY

If you can't hold target power, STOP the workout. 3 good intervals at 110% FTP are worth more than 5 intervals where the last two are at 95%. Bad reps don't count — they just create fatigue.

RULE 3: RECOVERY MEANS RECOVERY

Between intervals, drop to Zone 1. Not Zone 2, not "easy Zone 3." Zone 1. Full recovery ensures the next interval hits the right energy system.

RULE 4: CADENCE MATTERS

Alternate between high cadence (95-105 rpm) and low cadence (60-75 rpm) intervals. High cadence = cardiovascular stimulus. Low cadence = muscular strength. Gravel racing requires both — you need to grind up climbs and spin on flats.

Indoor vs Outdoor Workouts

Best done indoors: Short intervals (VO2max, anaerobic), FTP tests, cadence drills, recovery spins.

Best done outdoors: Long rides, tempo efforts, race simulations, skills practice, group rides.

Balance: Aim for at least 1-2 outdoor rides per week. Indoor training is efficient but doesn't prepare you for real-world race conditions — wind, terrain changes, group dynamics, and the mental challenge of being on the bike for 6+ hours.

When and How to Modify Workouts

  • Feeling great: Don't add volume or intensity. Stick to the plan. Save the good legs for the next hard day.
  • Feeling tired: Drop intensity by 5% but keep duration. If that's still too much, convert to Zone 2.
  • Feeling terrible: Convert to easy spin or take the day off. One missed workout doesn't matter. Two weeks of subpar training does.
  • Missed workout rule: Never try to make up a missed workout. Just move forward in the plan.

HRV: What It Is and How to Use It

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) measures the time between heartbeats. Higher variability = better recovery. Lower variability = accumulated stress.

  • Good for: Tracking long-term recovery trends (7-day rolling average), identifying overtraining before it happens
  • Not good for: Making single-day training decisions, replacing how you feel
  • How to use: Measure every morning before getting out of bed. If your 7-day average drops more than 10% below baseline, take an extra recovery day.
  • Apps: HRV4Training, Elite HRV, Whoop

Signs You Need More Recovery

  • Resting heart rate elevated 5+ bpm above normal
  • Can't hit power targets despite feeling "fine"
  • Motivation drops for more than 3 consecutive days
  • Sleep quality declining despite good sleep hygiene
  • Persistent muscle soreness beyond 48 hours
  • Getting sick more than usual

The Reality Check

The cycling nutrition industry wants you to believe you need seventeen different products, each with proprietary blend ratios and specific timing windows measured in seconds. You don't.

You need carbohydrates during exercise. You need protein and carbs after exercise. You need reasonable daily nutrition that supports training. That's 95% of it. Everything else is optimization you can worry about after you've nailed the basics.

The rule: The more frequently you train hard, the more critical recovery nutrition becomes.

Supplements

Most supplements are placebo at best, actively harmful at worst.

WORTH TAKING
  • Vitamin D (2000-4000 IU daily): Supports bone health, immune function, recovery. Most people are deficient. Get levels tested.
  • Creatine monohydrate (5g daily): Improves high-intensity repeatability. Useful for VO2max and sprint work. Cheap, well-researched, safe.
  • Caffeine (3-6mg/kg before hard sessions): Proven performance enhancer. Coffee works, pills work, gels work. Tolerance builds — cycle off occasionally.
NOT WORTH TAKING
  • BCAAs: Complete waste if you eat adequate protein
  • Testosterone boosters: Scams
  • Fat burners: Scams with side effects
  • Recovery drinks with proprietary blends: Overpriced protein + carbs

The rule: If you're deficient in something (Vitamin D, iron), fix it. If you're considering a supplement to “optimize,” ask yourself if you've already nailed sleep, nutrition basics, and training consistency. If not, fix those first.

Fueling During Workouts

This is where races are won or lost.

For any ride over 90 minutes at moderate-to-high intensity (Z3+), you need 60-80g of carbohydrates per hour. Your gut can absorb approximately 60g of glucose per hour through SGLT1 transporters. Add fructose (which uses different transporters) and you can push to 90g total. The target for most athletes is 70-75g per hour — enough to fuel hard efforts without GI distress.

Duration-Scaled Race Fueling

This is the key insight most nutrition advice gets wrong: carb intake should scale DOWN with race duration, not up. At lower intensities (longer races), your body shifts toward fat oxidation. Forcing 90g/hr of carbs into a system running at 44% VO2max exceeds physiological absorption capacity and causes GI distress.

Hydration

You lose 0.5-2 liters of fluid per hour depending on temperature, humidity, and effort. Dehydration of 2-3% bodyweight impairs performance — for you at 135.0 lbs, that is 1.2-1.8 liters.

  • Aim for: 500-750ml per hour (16-25 oz). More in heat, less in cold.
  • Short rides (<90 min): Water is fine
  • Long rides (2+ hours): Electrolyte drink with sodium (500-1000mg sodium/hour through sweat)
  • Don't overthink potassium, magnesium, or trace minerals. Sodium is 90% of what matters for performance.
CRAMPING

Cramps are NOT caused by electrolyte deficiency (despite what the supplement industry tells you). Cramps are caused by neuromuscular fatigue — your muscles are tired and misfiring. Salt tabs might help by changing neuromuscular excitability, but the real fix is better pacing and better training.

Training Your Gut

Your gut is trainable just like your muscles. If you never eat during training rides, your gut won't tolerate eating during races. SGLT1 transporters double in ~2 weeks of training (GSSI), but full adaptation takes 6-12 weeks.

  • Base Phase — Weeks 1-10 (Feb 14 – Apr 24): Practice eating real food on easy rides. 40-50g carbs/hour, mostly solid. Build tolerance gradually.
  • Build Phase — Weeks 11-16 (Apr 25 – Jun 5): Increase to 60-70g carbs/hour. Mix liquids and solids. Practice eating at tempo pace.
  • Peak Training — Weeks 17-18 (Jun 6 – Jun 19): Hit your race-day target. Race nutrition only (gels, drink, bars you'll use on race day). Practice at race pace.
  • Race Week (Jun 27): Stick with what worked in training. No new products. Trust your gut (literally).

Race-Day Nutrition Execution

Everything you practiced in training gets executed under stress.

PRE-RACE MEAL (3-4 HOURS BEFORE START)

Goal: Top off glycogen. Don't experiment.

  • 122-184g carbs
  • Moderate protein, low fat, low fiber
  • Familiar foods only
  • Examples: oatmeal with banana and honey, toast with jam and peanut butter, rice with eggs
  • Drink 500ml water with your meal
STARTING LINE (30-60 MIN BEFORE START)

Take one gel (24g carbs) with 200ml water. You're not adding meaningful glycogen — you're making sure blood glucose is stable as the race starts.

THE FUELING TIMELINE

The most common mistake: waiting until you're hungry to start eating. By the time you feel hungry, your glycogen is already depleted and your brain is glucose-starved. You're in a hole you can't climb out of.

Set a timer. Seriously. Your brain will be stupid. It will forget to eat. It will lie to you and say “I'm fine, I'll eat at the next aid station.” The timer removes decision-making.

WHEN YOUR STOMACH REBELS

It will. At some point in a long race, your gut will protest. High-intensity exercise diverts blood from your GI system to working muscles. Food sits there, undigested.

  1. Back off intensity for 5-10 minutes. Drop to Z2 pace. Let gut blood flow recover.
  2. Switch to liquid calories temporarily. Easier to digest than solids. Sports drink or cola at aid stations.
  3. Small sips, not big gulps. Easier on the stomach, less sloshing.
  4. Don't panic and stop eating entirely. You'll bonk 30 minutes later. Maintain some carb intake even if reduced.

What NOT to do: Hammer harder while nauseous. You'll either vomit or shut down completely.

Weight Management vs Performance

You want to be lean for racing. But chasing leanness during a training block is self-sabotage.

THE TRAINING BLOCK RULE

During your training build, your job is to train hard and recover well. Not cut weight. Energy deficit impairs recovery. Under-fueled training produces inferior workouts.

When to cut weight: After your race. During the off-season. When training volume is low and intensity is moderate. A 500-calorie daily deficit over 8-12 weeks can drop 4-6kg without destroying your fitness.

The rule: Chase performance metrics, not scale numbers. If your FTP and climbing times improve, your weight is fine. If they're declining, eat more.

THE RACE DAY FUELING RULE

On race day, eat what you've practiced. If you haven't tested a gel brand in training, don't eat it in the race. GI distress at mile 60 of a 100-mile race is a DNF-level problem that is 100% preventable.

13 · Mental Preparation

A 100-mile gravel race is as much a mental challenge as a physical one. Your body will want to quit before it needs to. Your brain will lie to you about how bad it is. Having a mental strategy is not optional.

THE DARK MILE

Around mile 80, most riders hit their lowest point. This is normal. It passes. Have a plan for it.

The 6-2-7 Breathing Technique

When things get hard (and they will), use this breathing pattern:

6s
Inhale
2s
Hold
7s
Exhale

Deep belly breath through the nose. Brief pause at the top. Slow, controlled exhale through the mouth. Longer exhale = parasympathetic activation.

When to use: Before the start, during long climbs, when you want to quit, at aid stations, during the dark mile.

Process Goals vs Outcome Goals

  • Bad goal: "Finish in under 8 hours." (You can't control weather, mechanicals, or how you feel.)
  • Good goal: "Fuel every 30 minutes. Stay in my zones. Ride my race." (100% within your control.)

On race day, focus exclusively on process goals. Check in every 30 minutes: Am I fueling? Am I hydrating? Am I in my zone? If yes, you're having a perfect race regardless of the clock.

Mantras That Work

Pick 2-3 short phrases and practice them in training. Say them out loud on hard efforts.

  • "This is what I trained for."
  • "One pedal stroke at a time."
  • "I've done harder things than this."
  • "Fuel, hydrate, breathe. Repeat."

The Quit Test

When you want to quit during the race (you will), ask yourself these three questions:

  1. Have I eaten in the last 30 minutes? If no, eat first. You might just be bonking.
  2. Have I been drinking? If no, drink 500ml. Dehydration mimics exhaustion.
  3. Am I actually injured or just uncomfortable? Discomfort is part of the race. Actual injury (sharp pain, swelling) is a reason to stop.

If you've eaten, hydrated, and aren't injured — keep going. The feeling will pass.

Race Execution Strategy

  • First 25% of race: Hold back. Ride 5% below target power/effort. Bank energy.
  • Middle 50% of race: Settle into target effort. Fuel relentlessly. Every 20-30 minutes.
  • Final 25% of race: If you feel good, increase effort slightly. If you're struggling, maintain. Do not blow up.

Start slower than you think you should. Every experienced gravel racer will tell you the same thing: the race doesn't start until the last third. Everyone goes out too hard. Be the one who doesn't.

In-Race Decision Tree

When things go wrong (and something always does), use these protocols:

IF: BONKING

Stop at next aid station. Eat 200+ calories solid food. Walk for 10 min. Resume at Zone 2 only.

IF: ALTITUDE SICKNESS

Reduce effort to Zone 1-2. Focus on breathing rhythm. If headache worsens, stop and descend.

IF: MECHANICAL

Move off course. Fix flat calmly. Check tire pressure for altitude (may need adjustment from sea level).

IF: THUNDERSTORM

If lightning: get low, off ridgeline. Do not shelter under lone trees. Wait it out - safety over finish time.

  • Eat a real meal within 60 minutes (protein + carbs + fat)
  • Light walking to keep blood flowing
  • No training for 3-5 days minimum

16 · Gravel Skills

Gravel racing rewards skill as much as fitness. A technically proficient rider at 3.5 W/kg will beat a 4.0 W/kg rider who can't handle loose corners or descents. These skills are trainable — but only if you practice them deliberately, not just ride through them.

Cornering on Loose Surfaces

THE WEIGHT SHIFT

On loose gravel, your front tire has less grip than on pavement. To corner safely:

  • Drop your outside foot to 6 o'clock position
  • Press weight through outside pedal and inside hand
  • Keep the bike more upright than you would on pavement
  • Look where you want to go — not at the obstacle you want to avoid
SPEED MANAGEMENT

All braking happens BEFORE the corner, not in it. On gravel:

  • Brake early and progressively — no grabbing
  • Release brakes before the apex
  • Use both brakes (70% front, 30% rear on hardpack; 50/50 on loose)
  • If the rear slides, do NOT grab the front brake

Descending on Gravel

BODY POSITION
  • Hands in the drops — maximum brake leverage and control
  • Elbows and knees slightly bent — act as suspension
  • Weight shifted back on steep descents (hips behind saddle)
  • One finger on each brake lever — maintain modulation
  • Eyes scanning 15-20 feet ahead, not right in front of the tire
LINE SELECTION
  • Smoother surface = faster, even if it's a longer line
  • Avoid loose marbles on hardpack — ride the packed line
  • On washboard: either go fast enough to float or slow enough to absorb
  • Ruts: commit to being in or out — straddling a rut is how you crash

Climbing on Gravel

TRACTION MANAGEMENT

Loose climbs are where most riders lose time. The key is steady, smooth power:

  • Stay seated as long as possible — standing reduces rear traction
  • Smooth pedal stroke — no surges, no mashing
  • Higher cadence (75-85 rpm) reduces torque spikes that break traction
  • Shift BEFORE you need to — shifting under load on gravel causes chain drops
WHEN TO STAND
  • Only on hardpack or very short steep pitches
  • When standing: keep weight forward, hands light on bars
  • Shift to a harder gear before standing to maintain momentum
  • If rear tire spins, sit immediately — you've lost traction

Tire Pressure for Gravel

Tire pressure is the single biggest performance variable on gravel — more impactful than any component upgrade you can buy.

GENERAL GUIDELINES
  • Tubeless: Always run tubeless for gravel. Period.
  • 38-42mm tires: 28-35 PSI for most riders
  • 45-50mm tires: 25-30 PSI for most riders
  • Lighter riders (<150 lbs): 2-4 PSI less than above
  • Heavier riders (>180 lbs): 2-4 PSI more than above
  • Rear tire: 2-3 PSI more than front (more weight)
CONDITIONS ADJUSTMENTS
  • Wet/muddy: Drop 2-3 PSI for grip
  • Dry hardpack: Can run 2-3 PSI higher for speed
  • Rocky/chunky: Drop 2-3 PSI to absorb impacts and prevent pinch flats
  • Mixed surface: Optimize for the worst surface you'll encounter
TEST YOUR PRESSURE BEFORE RACE DAY

Find your sweet spot by testing different pressures during long training rides. Drop 2 PSI at a time and note how the bike feels. Too low = squirmy in corners, too high = harsh and bouncy. The right pressure feels fast AND confident. Write it down. Use that pressure on race day.

Mechanical Skills

You must be self-sufficient on gravel. There are no team cars. Practice these before race day:

  • Flat repair: Fix a flat (tube or tubeless) in under 5 minutes. Practice at home.
  • Chain repair: Know how to use a chain tool. Carry a quick link.
  • Derailleur adjustment: Know how to adjust limit screws if your derailleur gets hit.
  • Tire plug: Practice plugging a tubeless tire with a plug kit. It takes 30 seconds once you've done it.
THE 5-MINUTE RULE

If you can't fix it in 5 minutes, you can't fix it on the course. The skills above cover 95% of mechanicals. The other 5% (cracked frame, destroyed wheel) require a ride to the nearest aid station. Carry a phone and know the course map.

17 · Altitude Training

SBT GRVL takes place at significant altitude. The start elevation is around 6732 feet, with racing at 7500+ feet. This has real, measurable effects on performance that you cannot ignore.

THE ALTITUDE REALITY

At 7500 feet, expect approximately 5.0% reduction in FTP compared to sea level. Your heart rate will run higher at the same power output. RPE becomes unreliable — use a power meter. Recovery between efforts takes longer. Sleep quality may suffer for the first 2-3 nights.

The Physiology

WHAT ALTITUDE DOES TO YOUR BODY
  • Reduced oxygen availability: At 7,000 ft, there's ~22% less oxygen per breath compared to sea level
  • Higher heart rate: Your heart pumps faster to compensate, running 5-15 bpm higher at same effort
  • Faster lactate accumulation: You reach threshold sooner with less absolute power
  • Impaired recovery: Between intervals, between efforts, between days — everything takes longer
  • Dehydration risk: Dry mountain air + increased respiration rate = faster fluid loss
  • Increased calorie burn: Your body works harder at altitude, burning 5-10% more calories

Power Loss by Elevation

Sea level - 3,000 ft0%No significant effect
3,000 - 5,000 ft1-2%Barely noticeable for most athletes
5,000 - 7,000 ft2-4%Noticeable on hard efforts
7,000 - 9,000 ft4-6%Significant — requires pacing adjustment
9,000 - 11,000 ft6-8%Major impact — acclimatization critical
11,000+ ft8-12%+Severe — multi-week acclimatization needed

Acclimatization Protocol

IF YOU CAN ARRIVE EARLY (IDEAL: 10-14 DAYS BEFORE)
  • Days 1-3: Easy activity only. Walk, light spin. Let your body adjust to breathing.
  • Days 4-7: Light training at reduced intensity (80% of normal power targets).
  • Days 8-14: Gradually return to normal training intensity. You'll feel closer to normal.
  • Key: Hydrate aggressively (3-4L/day), sleep extra, avoid alcohol.
IF YOU CAN'T ARRIVE EARLY (THE REALISTIC OPTION)

Most athletes can't take 2 weeks off before a race. Here's how to mitigate:

  • Arrive as late as possible — 24-48 hours before is better than 3-5 days. The worst window is 2-5 days at altitude (past the adrenaline boost, before any adaptation).
  • Heat training provides crossover benefits: 10-14 days of heat exposure increases plasma volume and hemoglobin, partially mimicking altitude adaptation.
  • Pace conservatively: Start 10% below target power. Altitude punishes going too hard early more than sea-level racing does.
  • Hydrate aggressively: Start hydrating 48 hours before the race. Drink 500ml+ more than normal daily.

Heat Acclimatization Protocol (Altitude Crossover)

Heat acclimatization delivers 5-8% performance improvements through plasma volume expansion, enhanced sweating, and reduced cardiovascular strain. Some of these adaptations directly help at altitude.

10-14 DAY HEAT PROTOCOL (WEEKS 6-10 OF YOUR PLAN)
  • Days 1-5: Fastest cardiovascular adaptations — plasma volume expands 4-15%, heart rate drops 8-20 bpm, sweating begins earlier.
  • Days 6-10: Thermoregulatory adaptations mature — sweat rate increases 10-25%, core temp drops 0.2-0.5°C during exercise.
  • Beyond 10-14 days: Hemoglobin mass may increase 3-4%, similar to altitude training benefits.

How to do it: Train indoors with the fan off or reduced. Wear extra layers. Sauna post-ride (15-20 min, working up gradually). These adaptations decay at ~2.5% per day without exposure.

Race Day at Altitude

ALTITUDE PACING RULES
  • Start 10% below your sea-level target power
  • Use power meter, not RPE — RPE is unreliable at altitude
  • Recovery between hard efforts takes 20-30% longer
  • Be judicious with matches early — altitude limits recovery
  • Fuel and hydrate more aggressively than at sea level (5-10% more calories, 500ml+ more fluid)
  • Expect your heart rate to be 5-15 bpm higher at the same power

18 · Women-Specific Considerations

If you're a woman training for gravel racing, your physiology is different from men's in ways that actually affect training and performance. This isn't patronizing — it's honest acknowledgment of real differences that matter.

The good news: these differences are trainable and manageable. The bad news: if you ignore them, you're making things harder than they need to be.

Menstrual Cycle & Training

Your menstrual cycle affects training capacity. Not as an excuse to skip workouts, but as a variable to monitor and work with.

FOLLICULAR PHASE (DAYS 1-14, START OF PERIOD TO OVULATION)

What's happening: Estrogen rises, body temperature is lower, insulin sensitivity is higher.

Training impact: This is typically your power window. You'll recover faster, handle intensity better, and feel stronger. Days 5-14 are often your best training days of the month.

How to use it: Schedule your hardest interval sessions, longest rides, and FTP tests during this phase when possible. Your body is primed for high-quality work.

LUTEAL PHASE (DAYS 15-28, OVULATION TO NEXT PERIOD)

What's happening: Progesterone dominates, body temperature rises (~0.5°F), metabolism shifts toward fat burning, inflammation increases.

Training impact: Recovery takes longer. Interval quality might decline. You might feel flat even when doing everything right. Heart rate runs 5-10 bpm higher at same effort. Carb cravings increase (because your body actually needs more carbs — progesterone reduces glycogen storage efficiency).

How to use it: This is not the time to test FTP or push for breakthrough sessions. Focus on base miles, maintenance intervals, and recovery. Listen to your body more carefully. If a training week falls during late luteal phase and you feel like garbage despite perfect execution, that's normal — it's hormones, not fitness loss.

IRON CONSIDERATIONS

Monthly blood loss means monthly iron loss. Women who menstruate need roughly 18mg of iron daily (vs. 8mg for men). Athletes need even more due to foot-strike hemolysis, GI losses, and increased red blood cell turnover.

Low iron = compromised training: Fatigue, inability to hit power targets, poor recovery, elevated heart rate, shortness of breath during efforts you should handle easily.

  • Get bloodwork annually (ferritin, serum iron, hemoglobin, hematocrit)
  • Target ferritin >50 ng/mL for athletes (many docs say >15 is "normal" — that's too low for performance)
  • Iron-rich foods: red meat, dark leafy greens, lentils, fortified cereals
  • Consider supplementation if levels are low (but get tested first — too much iron is also a problem)
  • Take iron with vitamin C (aids absorption), avoid taking with calcium (blocks absorption)

Your period is not an excuse to skip workouts, but it IS a variable to monitor. Track your cycle. Notice patterns. Adjust expectations during luteal phase. Capitalize on follicular phase. This is performance optimization, not weakness.

Fueling Differences

Women's bodies process fuel differently than men's, especially during exercise.

CARBOHYDRATE NEEDS

Women need MORE carbs relative to body weight than men. Despite often being told to eat less, female athletes actually need aggressive carbohydrate intake to support training and maintain hormonal health.

Why: Women's bodies preferentially spare carbohydrate and burn more fat at rest. Sounds great, except during high-intensity efforts (which is most of gravel racing), you NEED carbs. If you're chronically under-fueling carbs, your body will:

  • Downregulate thyroid function (slower metabolism, more fatigue)
  • Disrupt menstrual cycle (late periods, missed periods, longer cycles)
  • Compromise bone density
  • Tank performance

Your training day target: 367-428g carbs (61.2kg x 6-7g/kg). More on long ride days: 490-612g (61.2kg x 8-10g/kg).

Racing target: 60-80g carbs per hour for rides over 90 minutes at moderate-to-high intensity (scaled down for longer durations — see Nutrition Strategy section). Don't under-fuel trying to "stay lean" — that strategy kills performance AND health.

HEAT & HYDRATION

Women thermoregulate differently than men. This matters for gravel events.

Women sweat less than men at the same relative intensity. Sounds like an advantage (less fluid loss), but it's not — it means core temperature rises faster because evaporative cooling is less efficient.

The result: Women reach critical core temperature thresholds earlier in hot conditions, leading to earlier performance decline, higher perceived exertion at same power, greater cardiovascular strain, and increased risk of heat illness.

  • Pre-cooling is critical: Cold water immersion, ice vests, cold drinks before start
  • Aggressive cooling during race: Ice in jersey pockets, cold water over head/neck at aid stations
  • Heat acclimatization training: See the Altitude Training section — heat protocol provides critical benefits
  • Monitor core temp signals: Goosebumps, chills, confusion, nausea = pull back immediately

Recovery Needs

WHY WOMEN MAY NEED MORE RECOVERY

Women often need MORE recovery than men at the same relative training intensity. This isn't weakness — it's physiology.

  • Estrogen has anti-inflammatory properties, but it also affects tissue repair timing
  • Lower testosterone means slower muscle protein synthesis
  • Higher Type I muscle fiber percentage means different recovery demands
  • Hormonal fluctuations (especially luteal phase) increase baseline inflammation

What to do:

  • Pay closer attention to RHR and HRV trends
  • Don't try to match training partners if they're recovering faster — train YOUR body, not theirs
  • Prioritize sleep (7-9 hours minimum, non-negotiable)
  • Don't skip rest weeks
  • Back-to-back hard days might wreck you more — you might need an extra rest day per week

Pregnancy & Postpartum

IMPORTANT

This plan is NOT designed for pregnant or postpartum athletes.

If you're pregnant: Stop this plan immediately. Consult your physician before continuing ANY structured training. Racing is off the table, but moderate training may be safe with medical clearance.

Postpartum: Wait at least 6 months before attempting race-specific training. Get clearance from OB/GYN and ideally a pelvic floor physical therapist. Rebuild base fitness gradually — don't jump back into high-intensity work.

Equipment Considerations

  • Sports bra: Non-negotiable. Test on long rides, not just in the store. If it's uncomfortable for 30 minutes, it'll be unbearable at mile 100. High-impact support is essential — you're bouncing on gravel for hours.
  • Chamois: Women's anatomy requires women-specific design. This is not marketing — the padding placement and shape are different. A men's chamois will cause problems. Test multiple brands if needed. Numbness, chafing, or pain means wrong fit.
  • Saddle: Proper bike fit and saddle selection are critical. Women's sit bone width is often (but not always) wider than men's. Get a professional fit if you're experiencing numbness, pain, or discomfort. These issues don't resolve with more miles — they require equipment changes.

The Bottom Line

Your body is different. Not worse, not weaker — different. Train accordingly.

  • Track your cycle. Work with it, not against it.
  • Fuel aggressively. Don't under-eat trying to stay lean.
  • Take heat management seriously. You're at higher risk in hot conditions.
  • Prioritize recovery. You might need more than male training partners.
  • Get proper equipment. Generic gear won't cut it.

Racing gravel is hard for everyone. Being a woman doesn't make it harder — but ignoring female physiology makes it unnecessarily difficult. Do the work. Respect your body's signals. Show up prepared.

19 · Masters Training Considerations

At 44, your physiology is different from a 25-year-old's. Not worse — different. Understanding these differences is the key to training smarter and racing faster than athletes who ignore them.

Masters athletes can build exceptional race fitness. But the path there requires adjusting how you train, not how much.

THE MASTERS REALITY

Don't chase numbers from 10 years ago. Today's FTP is your training anchor. Progress is progress, regardless of absolute watts. The athlete who trains consistently at their current level will always beat the one who overreaches trying to recapture past glory.

What Changes After 40

RECOVERY

This is the single biggest change. Recovery takes longer — not dramatically, but meaningfully.

  • Hard session spacing: 48 hours minimum between high-intensity sessions (vs. 24-48 for younger athletes)
  • Recovery weeks: every 3 weeks (rather than the standard 4)
  • Sleep: 8+ hours is non-negotiable for adaptation. Your body no longer bounces back from 6-hour nights.
  • Two easy days between hard sessions is often optimal. Don't feel guilty about easy days — they're building fitness.
MUSCLE MASS & STRENGTH

Muscle mass declines approximately 1% per year after age 40. At 44, that's beginning to accelerate. This affects power output, bone density, metabolic rate, and injury resilience.

Strength training is not optional for masters athletes — it's mandatory.

  • 2x/week minimum during base phase, 1x/week during build/peak
  • Focus on: single-leg work (lunges, step-ups), deadlifts, squats, core stability
  • Heavy enough to challenge (6-10 rep range), not bodyweight circuits
  • Benefits beyond power: improved hormone regulation, insulin sensitivity, bone density, body composition

If you're 40+, strength training isn't optional — it's mandatory for long-term athletic health. Your future self — the one still racing at 50, 60, 70 — will thank you.

VO2MAX & CARDIOVASCULAR

VO2max declines ~1% per year after 30, but this rate is trainable. Masters athletes who maintain high-intensity training lose VO2max at roughly half the rate of sedentary peers.

  • Keep intensity in the plan: You need fewer hard sessions, but you still need them
  • Quality over quantity: 2 hard sessions/week is typically optimal (vs. 3-4 for younger athletes)
  • VO2max intervals remain critical: 3-5 minute efforts at 106-120% FTP maintain top-end fitness
  • Estimated HRmax at age 44: ~183 bpm (formula: 211 - 0.64 × age). Use actual measured max if available.

Training Approach: HRV-Guided Polarized

The recommended approach for masters athletes is polarized training with HRV-guided intensity: 80% easy, 20% hard, with recovery driving the schedule.

HOW THIS WORKS IN PRACTICE
  • Easy means truly easy. Zone 1-2 only. If you can't hold a conversation, you're going too hard. Polarized only works if easy days are truly easy.
  • Hard means genuinely hard. When you do go hard, commit fully. Zone 4-5. No "moderate" efforts.
  • Skip the gray zone. Zone 3 (tempo) is recovery-expensive for masters athletes. Use it strategically, not constantly. Threshold work costs more recovery than it's worth in most training weeks.
  • High cadence on intensity: 95-105 rpm on hard efforts reduces muscular strain and improves recovery. This matters more as you age — grinding at 70 rpm creates more muscle damage per watt.
  • Listen to HRV trends: If your 7-day HRV average drops more than 10% below baseline, convert the next hard session to easy. Your body is telling you it hasn't recovered.

THE MASTERS ADVANTAGE

You have something younger athletes don't: decades of experience, mental toughness, and the discipline to actually follow a plan. Masters athletes are typically more consistent, better at pacing, and less likely to do something stupid on race day. Your body may recover slower, but your brain makes better decisions. Use that.

Injury Prevention

  • Warmup is mandatory: 15-20 minutes progressive warmup before any intensity. Cold muscles + age = injury risk.
  • Mobility work: 10 minutes daily. Hip flexors, hamstrings, thoracic spine. Cycling creates tightness that compounds with age.
  • Don't ignore pain: "Working through it" is for 20-year-olds. At 44, persistent pain means something needs attention. Address it early or it becomes a season-ending problem.
  • Bike fit review: Bodies change. A fit from 5 years ago may no longer be optimal. Get reassessed annually.

The Bottom Line

Train smarter, not just harder. Prioritize recovery. Lift heavy things. Sleep more. Don't compare yourself to your 25-year-old self — compare yourself to other 44-year-olds, and you'll realize how far ahead you are just by having a structured plan.

The athletes who race well into their 50s, 60s, and 70s are the ones who respect what their body needs today, not what it could handle a decade ago.
