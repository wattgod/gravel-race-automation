# Training for Ultra-Distance Bikepacking Races

**A Gravel God Cycling white paper · July 2026 · v1 (owner review pending)**

Evidence base: `docs/research/ultra-bikepacking-coaching-market.md` (verified, cited)
plus Gravel God's existing coaching frameworks. Three evidence tiers are used and
labeled throughout: **[verified]** — claims that survived adversarial verification
against live sources; **[principle]** — established, uncontroversial exercise-science
reasoning; **[position]** — Gravel God's coaching stance, argued but not externally
proven. Nothing in this document claims first-person ultra-racing experience.

---

## 1. Thesis

Ultra-distance bikepacking races — Tour Divide, the Transcontinental, the Colorado
Trail Race, Atlas Mountain Race — do not require a different physiology than
single-day gravel racing. They require the same physiology prepared over a longer
horizon, defended by systems that single-day racing never tests.

The engine is the engine. What changes is everything around it:

1. **The horizon.** Preparation stretches from twelve weeks to six-to-nine months,
   not because adaptation works differently, but because the scope of preparation is
   wider and base fitness cannot be crash-built.
2. **The demand.** The race stops asking "how fast for one day?" and starts asking
   "how much, every day, without breaking?" Repeatability replaces peak output as the
   organizing goal.
3. **The failure modes.** Finishing is decided less by fitness than by logistics,
   sleep, fueling economics, tissue tolerance, and the mind at day five. These are
   trainable, and mostly untrained.

This paper describes how we would prepare an athlete for these races: the same
durability-first training model we use for events like Unbound, extended in horizon
and surrounded by four new preparation tracks — logistics, mental protocols,
nutrition economics, and structural durability.

## 2. What the demand actually is

A single-day gravel race like Unbound 200 asks for 8–14 hours of output, once,
followed by unlimited recovery. An ultra asks for something categorically different
in shape even if identical in substrate: a Tour Divide racer targeting a 24-day
finish must average roughly 100 miles and 5,500 feet of climbing per day, every day,
on a loaded bike **[verified]** — with recovery compressed into whatever sleep the
racer's own strategy allows.

Three consequences follow **[principle]**:

- **Intensity ceilings compress.** Nobody rides threshold intervals across Montana.
  Racing intensity lives almost entirely in the moderate and easy domains; the
  athlete's high-end matters only insofar as it raises the ceiling that an all-day
  pace draws against. A rider whose easy pace is another rider's tempo wins by
  physiology arithmetic, not heroics.
- **Durability becomes the whole game.** The performance question is not power at
  hour one but power — and posture, and appetite, and judgment — at hour ten of day
  six. Fatigue resistance, already the center of our gravel training model, stops
  being one quality among several and becomes the organizing quality.
- **Recovery becomes an in-race skill.** In single-day racing, recovery is what you
  do after. In ultras, recovery is executed *during* the race — sleep systems, refeed
  strategy, tissue management — and can be trained and planned like intervals.
  The community treats in-race sleep strategy as a discrete, learnable skill with
  published doctrine (bivvy/hotel selection, no-booking-ahead rules, testing the
  sleep system before the race) **[verified]**.

## 3. Why the timeline changes even though the physiology doesn't

The market evidence is unambiguous: no credible vendor sells a 12-week ultra
preparation product. Verified practitioner formats are 4- and 6-month static plans
(with a base-fitness prerequisite on top), 6-month coaching minimums, and a
recommended nine months for Tour Divide from the coach whose program is built around
it **[verified]**.

Our reading of *why* — consistent with that evidence but going beyond it
**[position]**:

1. **Base debt can't be crash-paid.** The aerobic volume that supports repeated
   150-mile days is accumulated in months of consistent riding, not compressed
   blocks. A 12-week window forces athletes to play catch-up, which for this demand
   profile means arriving underbuilt or arriving injured. The single biggest
   difference from Unbound preparation is not the content of training but the
   refusal to compress it.
2. **The preparation scope is wider than training.** The horizon must also fund
   route research, gear and sleep-system selection and testing, resupply planning,
   and mental preparation. The nine-month Tour Divide recommendation explicitly
   covers this non-fitness content **[verified]** — the calendar is long because the
   checklist is long, not because adaptation is slow.
3. **Simulation needs runway.** Multi-day loaded test blocks (Section 4) are
   themselves large training stresses that need recovery windows around them. They
   don't fit inside twelve weeks alongside a base build.

## 4. The training model

The engine of our existing gravel model carries over intact: mostly-easy intensity
distribution, progressive long-ride durability work, race-demand-driven session
selection, and adaptation decisions made on trends rather than single-day events.
What follows is that model stretched onto the ultra demand profile. (Where the
research verified anything about competitors' training specifics it is marked; the
verification pass killed most claims about ultra intensity-distribution doctrine, so
this section is predominantly our model applied to the demand — **[position]**
throughout unless marked.)

**Phase structure across 6–9 months:**

1. **Base (months 1–3, longer for less-trained athletes).** High-frequency easy
   volume; the goal is a chassis that absorbs everything later. Opens with baseline
   testing — the same testing-week protocol our base plans use, with RPE calibration
   mattering more than power benchmarks here, because ultras are paced by feel and
   judgment, not by a head unit target.
2. **Durability build (months 3–5).** The long ride progresses in duration and in
   *state*: long rides fed normally, then long rides at the back half of big weeks,
   then back-to-back long days. Fatigue-resistance sessions — quality work placed
   deep into long rides — carry over directly from our gravel library. A limited
   amount of higher-intensity work is retained to hold the ceiling up, on the
   arithmetic argument from Section 2, not because the race asks for it directly.
3. **Specificity and simulation (months 5–7).** The distinctive block. Loaded,
   consecutive long days on terrain resembling the race; at least one 2–4 day
   simulation ridden with full race systems — the pack, the sleep kit, the planned
   resupply cadence, the planned daily schedule. The simulation's job is only
   partly physiological: it is the systems shakedown that turns gear lists and
   spreadsheets into tested doctrine, and it is where sleep strategy gets rehearsed
   before it is executed for real. Practitioners test sleep systems before racing
   them **[verified]**; we extend the same logic to every system.
4. **Consolidation and taper (final 4–8 weeks).** Volume consolidates; the athlete
   finishes preparation rested, with every system already decided. Nothing new —
   no new gear, no new fueling, no new route theories — enters after this point.
5. **Adaptation policy across all phases.** On a multi-month horizon, reacting to
   individual bad days is noise-chasing. We adjust on trends — rolling fatigue,
   sleep, life load — exactly as our standard coaching model prescribes, with the
   dial turned further toward damping because the horizon is longer.

## 5. Structural durability: strength and mobility for the position

At ultra duration, the limiter that actually ends races is frequently structural,
not metabolic: hands, neck, feet, contact points, low back — the tissues holding the
position hour after hour, day after day. Commercial ultra preparation treats
"comfort systems" — bike fit, contact-point protection, hygiene protocols — as core
curriculum on the stated thesis that pain ends races **[verified]**.

Our strength programming already targets sustainability in the riding position;
for ultra preparation it is not an accessory but a parallel track **[position]**:

- Year-round, low-dose strength work biased toward trunk endurance, grip and
  forearm capacity, and hip/posterior-chain resilience under fatigue.
- Mobility work aimed at tolerating — not just achieving — the ride position,
  including neck endurance for days of sustained extension.
- Bike fit treated as a training variable: the fit that is fast for eight hours and
  the fit that is survivable for eight days are different fits, and the simulation
  block exists partly to find out which one the athlete actually has.

## 6. Nutrition: from fueling a race to running an energy economy

Single-day fueling is a solved input problem: hit a carbohydrate target per hour,
manage gut tolerance, finish. Ultra nutrition is an economy: daily expenditures in
the many thousands of calories, met from whatever gas stations and cafés the route
supplies, under appetite suppression that compounds with fatigue **[principle]**.
Verified vendor curricula give nutrition its own chapters and consult coverage
**[verified]**.

Preparation therefore covers three layers **[position]**:

1. **In-ride fueling** — the same per-hour discipline as gravel racing, trained in
   long rides until it is automatic at low motivation.
2. **Daily replenishment** — planned around resupply reality: what can actually be
   bought, carried, and digested, rehearsed in the simulation block rather than
   improvised on day three.
3. **Appetite management** — treating eating as a scheduled task rather than a
   response to hunger, because hunger stops being a reliable signal precisely when
   the deficit is largest.

## 7. The mind, prepared in advance

The most striking verified finding in the research is a mental-preparation doctrine:
the riders who fail to finish are rarely the least fit; leading preparation includes
pre-written decision rules — quitting criteria written months in advance for the
version of the athlete "that can no longer think straight" **[verified]**.

This matches the mechanism at the center of our coaching model: the athlete is their
own worst blindspot, and knowledge isn't the limiter, application is. An ultra
amplifies that mechanism to its extreme — by day five, the athlete's judgment is
itself the degraded system. Our preparation applies the same tools we already use,
scaled up **[position]**:

- **Pre-commitment documents**: quitting rules, continue-rules, and scratch
  criteria agreed between coach and athlete before the race, so the decision is
  made by the rested mind, not the broken one.
- **Segmented goal structure**: the race is planned as resupply-to-resupply
  segments, never as a whole, because "1,800 miles to go" is not a usable thought.
- **Rehearsed low points**: the simulation block deliberately includes riding
  through a scheduled bad patch — underslept, behind on calories — so the first
  encounter with the state is not in Montana.
- **A human in the loop**: where race rules permit contact, scheduled coach
  check-ins function as external judgment; where they don't, the pre-commitment
  documents stand in for it.

## 8. Logistics as curriculum

Every verified commercial ultra program packages non-training preparation as core
content, not as an add-on: gear and pack systems, route research and road-book
construction, resupply mapping, sleep-system selection **[verified]**. For
free-route races like the Transcontinental, route design between controls is itself
a competitive discipline with published community doctrine **[verified]**.

Two implications for preparation **[position]**:

1. Logistics work is *scheduled*, with the same seriousness as training blocks:
   route study sessions, gear-decision deadlines, resupply spreadsheets built and
   then field-tested in simulation. The deadline structure exists precisely because
   this work is unbounded and procrastination-prone.
2. Race-demand analysis — the discipline of reading a course's terrain, climate,
   altitude, and failure points before building the plan — extends naturally from
   our existing per-race methodology. The unit of analysis changes from "where does
   this race break apart?" to "where does each *day* of this race break apart, and
   what does that do to the days after it?"

## 9. What transfers from single-day gravel practice, and what doesn't

| Component | Transfer |
|---|---|
| Durability-first training model, fatigue-resistance sessions | Direct — becomes the organizing principle |
| Mostly-easy intensity distribution | Direct — with intensity's role reframed as ceiling maintenance |
| Race-demand analysis | Direct method, new unit of analysis (per-day, compounding) |
| Testing-week baselines, RPE calibration | Direct — RPE weighting increases |
| Trend-based adaptive coaching, weekly review | Direct — damping increased for the longer horizon |
| In-ride fueling discipline | Direct — becomes layer one of three |
| Strength for position sustainability | Direct — promoted from accessory to parallel track |
| 12-week plan format | Does not transfer — no verified vendor sells one; horizon is 6–9 months **[verified]** |
| Taper-and-peak single-day scheduling | Does not transfer — replaced by repeatability consolidation |
| Recovery as a post-race concern | Does not transfer — recovery becomes an in-race skill |
| Coach's first-person race experience | Gap — verified vendors are finishers/winners of these events; our authority here is method and evidence, and our copy must never imply otherwise |

## 10. Honest limits of this paper

- The adversarial verification pass refuted most specific claims about competitors'
  ultra training methodology (exact intensity distributions, sleep-deprivation
  training protocols). Section 4 is therefore our model applied to a verified demand
  profile — a coaching position, not a settled literature. We state it plainly
  rather than dressing it in borrowed citations.
- Sleep-deprivation *training* (riding deliberately sleep-deprived in preparation)
  is deliberately excluded: we found no verified evidence for its safety or
  efficacy, and its risk profile is obvious. Our model rehearses managed low points
  and in-race sleep *strategy*, not chronic sleep restriction.
- No one at Gravel God has raced Tour Divide. Everything logistical in this paper
  is sourced from verified practitioner doctrine and community-published guides,
  not first-person experience, and any product built on it must say so.
