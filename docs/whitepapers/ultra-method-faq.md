# Questions People Will Actually Ask About the Gravel God Ultra-Bikepacking Method and Plans

## What makes this different from a longer gravel plan?

The training engine is deliberately familiar: mostly easy riding, progressive long rides, strength for the position, and a small amount of quality work. The difference is the target. A one-day event asks what a rider can produce once; an ultra asks what can be repeated day after day on a loaded bike while appetite, comfort, judgment, and logistics are under pressure.

That changes the calendar as much as the workouts. The method includes loaded back-to-backs, a full-systems simulation, planned recovery, fueling practice, and preparation tasks for gear, resupply, navigation, sleep systems, and decision-making. This is a coaching position built around a verified demand profile, not a claim that ultra physiology is a separate science.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §§3–5, 8–10; White Paper §§1–4 [verified], [principle], [position]; https://bikepackingcoach.cc/race-plan-tour-divide-2027

## Have you actually raced Tour Divide?

No one at Gravel God has raced Tour Divide. The method does not imply otherwise, and it does not use a borrowed first-person story as proof.

Its authority is narrower and more inspectable: a documented training method, a race-demand model, published practitioner guidance, and a race database that treats each event’s climbing, terrain, altitude, heat, and navigation demands as inputs. That does not erase the credibility gap between a method-led program and coaching from a Tour Divide finisher. It means the gap should be named plainly. Where the evidence supports a claim, it is cited. Where the method makes a coaching judgment, it is labeled as a position rather than dressed up as settled science.

Sources: Bikepacking Program Spec v2.2, Hard Rules R1–R5; White Paper §9 [position], §10 [position]; https://ultramtb.net/about/; https://bikepackingcoach.cc/race-plans

## Why is there no 12-week ultra plan?

Because twelve weeks is not presented as full ultra preparation. The verified market pattern is longer: four- and six-month static plans with base prerequisites, longer coaching commitments, and a nine-month Tour Divide recommendation from one specialist. The reason is not simply that fitness takes time. The preparation also needs time for loaded riding, systems testing, route work, gear decisions, resupply planning, and recovery after simulation blocks.

A rider with less than sixteen weeks is not ignored. The custom pathway can provide an honestly framed catch-up preparation from six to fifteen weeks when appropriate. It prioritizes what fits and states what cannot be built in time. It is not titled or sold as a 12-week ultra plan.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §§1, 12.3; Bikepacking Program Spec v2.2, R2; White Paper §3 [verified], [position]; https://www.ultramtb.net/trainingplans.html; https://bikepackingcoach.cc/race-plan-tour-divide-2027

## Thirteen to fourteen peak hours seems low for a 20-day race. Is that enough?

Peak training hours are not meant to imitate race-week hours. Trying to match a 20-day event’s daily load before the start would mostly create fatigue, injury risk, and a compromised taper. The plan instead builds repeatability through progressive weekly volume, loaded long rides, back-to-back days, fueling practice, and a multi-day simulation. The longest 24-week standard load weeks reach 13 hours before the simulation and 14 hours after it; the simulation itself reaches 18 moving hours across three consecutive days.

That is a coaching position, not a proven universal dose. The plan is designed for a working athlete, with weekend volume carrying much of the load and weekdays generally capped at 90 minutes. A rider whose current capacity or race ambition requires substantially more should not assume that more is automatically better; that is a case for individual review.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §2, §§4–5, §11A; White Paper §§2, 4 [verified], [principle], [position]; https://sarahjswallow.com/stories/2021/5/18/how-i-am-preparing-for-the-tour-divide

## Why include VO2 work for an ultra?

VO2 work means short, very hard efforts aimed at maintaining the top end of aerobic capacity. An ultra is not raced at that intensity. The argument for retaining a small dose is indirect: all-day pace sits below an athlete’s ceiling, and a ceiling left entirely untouched may decline.

In this method, VO2 is maintenance, not the center of the program. Base and build phases include at least one VO2 session in each rolling 21-day period; it disappears during simulations and the taper. The larger share of quality work is fatigue resistance: controlled work placed late in a long ride, when posture, fueling, and judgment are already being tested.

This is explicitly a coaching position. Research did not establish a definitive ultra-specific intensity formula, so the plan does not claim one.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §3, §13.11; White Paper §§2, 4 [principle], [position], §10 [position]; https://www.ultramtb.net/trainingplans.html

## Why use RPE instead of power targets?

RPE is rate of perceived exertion, on a 1–10 scale. It is the primary guide because an ultra rider has to pace through terrain, weather, altitude, load, sleep disruption, stops, and changing equipment. A power number can still be useful, but it is not a command that overrides what the rider is actually experiencing.

The method uses power and heart rate as secondary information. RPE makes the prescription portable to riders without a power meter and more relevant to the race itself, where a loaded climb, rough descent, or poor night of sleep can make identical wattage mean very different things. The first testing week and later retests help calibrate effort, but the desired outcome is better judgment, not obedience to a head unit.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §§2–3, §7; White Paper §4 [position], §9 [position]; https://www.ultramtb.net/trainingplans.html

## Why will you not train me on sleep deprivation?

Because deliberate sleep restriction in training is excluded. The evidence review did not establish that it is safe or effective, and the downside is obvious: poor recovery, impaired judgment, and greater risk during riding. The method does not confuse rehearsing race logistics with manufacturing sleep loss.

Sleep strategy is still part of preparation. Riders can test sleep systems, practice deploying and packing them, learn what a bivvy or hotel stop requires, and develop decision rules for when to rest during the event. A simulation may include an overnight when the race requires bivvy competence, but sleep duration is never restricted. The low point being rehearsed is accumulated fatigue and fueling discipline, not intentional sleep deprivation.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §5, §13.8; Bikepacking Program Spec v2.2, R4; White Paper §§4, 7, 10 [verified], [position]; https://dotwatcher.cc/features/guides

## How can a two- or three-day simulation prepare me for a race that lasts weeks?

It cannot reproduce the whole event. That is not its job. A simulation is a concentrated systems test: loaded consecutive days, realistic fueling, pack balance, contact points, navigation, resupply timing, sleep-system handling, and the friction of starting again when tired. It exposes weak assumptions while there is still time to change them.

The method uses two days in a 16-week plan, three in a 24-week plan, and up to four in longer custom plans. The goal is not to accumulate the most punishing weekend possible. It is to leave with usable answers: what rubs, what is hard to reach, what food fails, what navigation setup breaks down, and what slows the next morning.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §5, §5.1; White Paper §4 [verified], [position], §8 [verified], [position]; https://dotwatcher.cc/features/guides

## Why is the week after the simulation easy?

Because the simulation is a major training stress, not a final exam that must be “earned” by training through it. The plan makes the following week a recovery week, with the first four days limited to rest or recovery spins and no intensity. Masters riders receive a five-day window.

That recovery is part of the simulation prescription, not an optional concession. The method needs the athlete to absorb the work, resolve contact-point or equipment issues, and enter the remaining specificity and taper weeks with usable fitness. Adding hard work immediately afterward would make the plan look more aggressive without necessarily making it more effective. The contract treats the recovery window as a hard quality-control requirement.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §5, §6, §13.4; White Paper §3 [position], §4 [position]; https://www.ultramtb.net/trainingplans.html

## I only have eight hours a week. Can I do this?

Eight hours can support meaningful preparation if the starting base is appropriate and the race expectation is realistic. The early and middle parts of the standard plans sit around that range, but peak weeks, long rides, back-to-backs, and the simulation still require protected time. The method is designed around a normal workweek: weekend long rides, weekdays mostly under 90 minutes, trainer sessions where useful, commute miles counted normally, and some aerobic work split into two 45-minute sessions.

Below six available hours per week, the method says Tour Divide-class preparation cannot be honestly delivered on any timeline. Between six and the prescribed hours, the plan reduces midweek volume first, then standalone intensity; it protects the long day, simulation, and recovery weeks as long as possible.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §§1–2, §11, §11A; White Paper §§3–4 [position]; https://bikepackingcoach.cc/race-plan-tour-divide-2027

## What is different for Masters riders and riders over 50?

The Masters version does not assume that age alone determines ability. It changes recovery architecture. Recovery weeks occur every third training week rather than every fourth, those weeks contain no quality sessions, and the post-simulation recovery window is five days rather than four. The simulation volume and key long-day structure remain, but the build includes more deliberate opportunities to absorb work.

Strength remains part of the plan because trunk, neck, grip, posterior-chain, and positional durability matter over long days. It is not placed immediately before long rides or back-to-backs, and it drops out during simulation weeks and the final two weeks. The point is not to make a softer plan. It is to keep the load structure viable enough to complete consistently.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §2, §§5–6, §8; White Paper §5 [verified], [position]; https://www.acier.cc/full-season

The listed prices are $129 for 16 weeks and $199 for 24 weeks. The lower competitor prices are real: UltraMTB’s static plans are listed at $65 and $75. Price alone does not prove comparable scope or quality.

The intended difference is product structure: race-specific variations, a 16- or 24-week calendar, loaded back-to-backs, a required simulation and recovery window, strength continuity, a hosted preparation guide, and quality checks against the method contract. That is the rationale, not a claim that the plans are objectively worth more for every rider. A rider deciding between them should compare duration, prerequisites, guide depth, race specificity, and whether a static plan is sufficient for their situation.

## My race is in 12 weeks. What happens?

The honest answer is that full preparation is not possible in twelve weeks. If there are six to fifteen weeks remaining, the custom pathway can produce a catch-up preparation rather than pretending the athlete is on a normal ultra build. It begins from current training history, progresses under the same ramp and recovery limits, preserves a two-day simulation when there are at least ten weeks, and ends with a taper.

The welcome framing must identify what the abbreviated timeline prioritizes and what it omits relative to a full build. The race should be approached conservatively. If the rider lacks the basic history of consistent riding, available time, or a recent long ride, the right answer may be to defer, change expectations, or use the event as reconnaissance rather than force a heroic plan.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §1, §12.3; Bikepacking Program Spec v2.2, R2 and WS-C §6; White Paper §3 [verified], [position]; https://bikepackingcoach.cc/race-plan-tour-divide-2027

## Do I need to sleep outside during training?

Not automatically. An overnight is encouraged when the target race requires bivvy competence, because sleep-system deployment, packing, warmth, and morning-start friction are practical skills. But the method does not make sleeping outside a blanket requirement, and it never restricts sleep to make training more “real.”

For a race where hotels, indoor stops, or supported indoor sleep are the likely strategy, training should test the systems that match that reality. For a race that may require a bivvy, a controlled overnight can reveal whether the bag, pad, layers, and packing process actually work. The value is in reducing uncertainty before the race, not collecting hardship.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §5, §5.1; White Paper §§4, 8 [verified], [position], §10 [position]; https://dotwatcher.cc/features/guides

## Does the plan tell me what bike, bags, and gear to buy?

It should not pretend that a generic training calendar can select every piece of equipment for every rider and route. The plan’s role is to create repeated, loaded opportunities to test the equipment already under consideration: pack balance, contact points, access to food, lighting, sleep-system handling, and the speed of morning setup.

The accompanying guide covers the preparation process: gear decisions, bike setup, comfort systems, resupply, and field-testing them in simulation. Race-specific plan variations can add bounded demands such as climbing scale, technical handling blocks, altitude or heat notes, and free-route tasks. A complex bike, fit, or luggage problem may still need an individual consult or qualified fitter rather than a calendar note.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §§4–5, §10; White Paper §§5, 8 [verified], [position]; https://bikepackingcoach.cc/race-plan-tour-divide-2027; https://www.acier.cc/coaching-consultations

## Will it teach navigation and route planning?

It covers navigation as a preparation skill, not as a replacement for race rules, official route files, mapping tools, or local knowledge. The simulation checklist includes navigation trust: testing the device, power plan, files, mounting, and the rider’s ability to recover from a wrong turn without turning it into a crisis.

For fixed-route events, the practical work is route familiarization and reliability. For free-route events, route research becomes a larger curriculum item. The race-variation framework can add route-research tasks and a route-plan validation step during specificity weeks. That is appropriate because free-route navigation is a distinct learned discipline, but it should not be mistaken for a universal route prescription.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §5.1, §10; White Paper §8 [verified], [position]; https://dotwatcher.cc/feature/follow-your-own-adventure

## What does mental preparation mean here?

It means making some decisions while rested, before fatigue, poor sleep, discomfort, and a long stretch of bad luck narrow judgment. The method includes segmented goals, pre-written continue and stop criteria, resupply-to-resupply thinking, and rehearsed low points involving fatigue and fueling rather than deliberate sleep loss.

This is not a claim that a document can solve every emotional or safety decision in an ultra. Conditions change, and medical or safety decisions remain situational. The value is simpler: reduce the number of important decisions first made by the version of the rider who is exhausted and discouraged. When race rules allow support contact, check-ins can help; when they do not, the preparation documents need to stand on their own.

Sources: White Paper §7 [verified], [position], §10 [position]; Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §5, §9; https://bikepackingcoach.cc/race-plan-tour-divide-2027

## What does the guide cover that the training plan does not?

The training plan is the calendar: rides, intensity placement, strength, loaded days, simulation, recovery, and race-start taper. The guide is the wider curriculum that a calendar cannot fully carry: race selection, systems, energy economy, mental preparation, logistics, and simulation/race-week decision-making.

It is delivered as a hosted guide link in a Day 1 calendar note rather than a PDF attachment. The guide is intended to cite claims by evidence tier or source URL and to avoid claims of personal ultra-racing experience. It is useful context for the plan, but it does not replace a medical professional, a bike fit assessment, official race materials, or individual coaching where the athlete’s situation needs judgment beyond the plan’s rules.

Sources: Bikepacking Program Spec v2.2, WS-G §2 and WS-P §3–5; White Paper §§5–8 [verified], [position]; Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §§5, 8–10

## How race-specific are these plans?

The base structure is race-neutral, then a race changes only defined inputs rather than receiving an entirely invented method. Those inputs can include climbing scale, technical handling blocks, altitude, heat, free-route tasks, and a recommendation bias toward 16 or 24 weeks. Tour Divide, Colorado Trail Race, and Badlands are the initial race-specific variations named in the product specification.

That keeps the core logic consistent while acknowledging that a loaded, high-altitude, technical route is not the same preparation problem as a hotter or less technical one. It also sets a limit: race-specific does not mean every road surface, weather event, resupply closure, or individual equipment choice can be predicted by the plan.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §10; Bikepacking Program Spec v2.2, WS-P §2; White Paper §§2, 8–9 [verified], [position]

## When should I choose 16 weeks versus 24 weeks?

Choose based on runway and readiness, not impatience. Sixteen weeks is the shorter complete structure for a rider who meets the prerequisites: consistent riding, a rolling four-week average of at least six hours, a recent four-hour ride, and no current condition that limits consecutive riding days. Twenty-four weeks provides more base and build runway, includes a mini-simulation before the main simulation, and is generally the more conservative choice for a longer or more demanding target.

A rider who does not meet the prerequisites but has at least sixteen weeks enters with reduced early volume and delayed back-to-backs rather than being pushed into the canonical workload. More than 36 weeks uses a hold-then-build approach rather than an endlessly escalating plan.

Sources: Ultra Method Contract v2.6 §§1–2, §5, §12.1–12.2; White Paper §3 [verified], [position]; https://www.ultramtb.net/trainingplans.html

## Can I get a custom plan instead of a fixed 16- or 24-week plan?

Yes, where the target and runway fit the custom rules. Custom ultra preparation can be generated for 16 to 36 weeks, with the same phase logic, recovery cadence, simulation requirements, taper structure, and race-demand deltas as the standard plans. It can adapt to actual weekly availability by reducing midweek volume before reducing standalone intensity, then adjusting back-to-backs.

Custom does not mean unconstrained. Below six weeks, no automatic plan is appropriate. Below sixteen weeks, the result is catch-up preparation rather than a relabeled ultra plan. Below six available hours per week, Tour Divide-class preparation is not presented as an honest fit. A medical limitation that affects consecutive riding triggers human review before any plan is built.
