The most popular training zone in cycling started as a doodle.

In 2004, Andy Coggan — legitimately one of the smartest people in power-based training — sketched a hypothetical, unitless graph of training efficiency versus intensity. No data on it. By his own description, “only ever intended as conveying a concept.” A coach named Frank Overton looked at that napkin scratch, named its peak “the Sweet Spot,” attached 88–94% of FTP to it, and built a 20-year business on the result.

A generation of cyclists internalized that riding at Sweet Spot is the hack that makes you faster, faster. The opposite is true — not because I'm a convincing person, but scientifically, repeatedly, demonstrably.

I wrote the whole case — the origin story (which is genuinely funny), the physiology, and what to do instead:

Sweet Spot Isn't That Sweet

If you've been doing Tuesday-night Sweet Spot intervals all winter, I'm sorry in advance.
