A math problem: an 8-hour gravel race at 75g of carbs an hour is 600 grams of carbohydrate. That's 24 gels. Twenty-four.

Almost nobody carries that, which is why the most common way to waste six months of training isn't fitness. It's arithmetic.

The four people who bonk at mile 80 (I have personally been all four):

The Optimist. “Aid stations will have what I need.” The first 500 riders cleaned out the bananas at station 3. You're rider 1,200.

The Minimalist. “I'll eat when I'm hungry.” Hunger is a late-stage bonk signal. Fuel by the clock — something every 20 minutes, no negotiation.

The Experimenter. “New gel flavor, race day, what could go wrong.” Mile 60 is what could go wrong.

The Denier. “I don't want to carry that much.” The math doesn't care.

“I've ridden centuries on two Clif bars and a gas-station Coke. I was fine.”

Touché — at endurance pace, fat stores carry most of the load and you can get away with it. A gravel race is a different animal: the intensity is higher, the carb burn roughly doubles, and “fine” quietly becomes “walking a climb at mile 85 doing sad math.” You're not fueling today's ride. You're fueling the last two hours of it.

The fix is unglamorous: your gut is trainable, like a muscle. Start at 40–50g an hour on easy rides — the transporters that move carbs out of your stomach can roughly double their capacity in two weeks of consistent practice. Build to your race rate by your peak weeks. Then race on exactly what you trained on. Nothing new on race day. Ever.

The full protocol — targets by ride type, the gut-training timeline, what to do when it all goes sideways anyway — is in chapter 5 of the guide: Nutrition & Fueling. Free, no gate.
