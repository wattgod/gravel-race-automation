## Equipment & Gear Recommendations

**Bike Choice:**

**Shona & Rich / Keep Pedalling [COMPETITIVE]:** Recommended Salsa Cutthroats: "They're the perfect bikes for the race, fatter tyres for soaking up the off road stuff, light enough to let us get a move on on the road sections. The 'Cutty is super comfy for long hours in the saddle (the Class V vibration technology really does work) plus they have great capacity for carrying stuff." (https://advntr.cc/atlas-mountain-race-countdown/)

**Justinas Leveika & Seb Breuer [ELITE]:** "It's better to be over-biked than under-biked. Full suspension will make you enjoy the route more. This year's winner, Robin Gemperle, rode a lightweight Scott Spark full-suspension bike. A hardtail with front suspension is, in my opinion, a racy choice. And if you choose a gravel bike – you really like to put yourself through the pain." (https://www.tailfin.cc/blog/10-lessons-learned-from-the-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Seb Breuer [ELITE]:** "For me, a mountain bike is a perfect choice regarding the terrain ridden and your comfort. For sure, a mountain bike with aerobars is a bit strange looking, but who cares if you are fast and comfortable in your position!" (https://www.tailfin.cc/blog/10-lessons-learned-from-the-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Kevin 'Benky' Benkenstein [ELITE]:** "In my post-race assessment back then, I had resolved that comfort over time was more important than speed on one climb, and also that reliability of parts was all important." (https://escapecollective.com/bike-check-surviving-the-1-300-km-atlas-mountain-race/)

**James Mark Hayden [COMPETITIVE]:** "Yes, you would be best riding a mountain bike. In 2020, I was one of very few people that ran a hardtail mountain bike, with 100mm suspension and 2.25 Mezcals. I was very comfortable and thus fast. I had no saddle sores or issues and flew down descents." (https://jamesmarkhayden.eu/knowledge/tips-on-how-to-complete-the-atlas-mountain-race)

**Marei Moldenhauer [UNKNOWN]:** "I knew one thing for sure: I wasn't riding this on a gravel bike. I'd rather be overbiked than underbiked, and a gravel bike would have been way too underbiked for this race—that just wasn't an option. So, I needed a mountain bike." (https://cyclite.cc/en/blogs/news/atlas-mountain-race-marei-moldenhauer)

**Tires:**

**Shona & Rich / Keep Pedalling [COMPETITIVE]:** "Terravail Sparwoods or Maxxis Ikons. Fast rolling XC tyres with decent puncture protection and you won't go wrong." (https://advntr.cc/atlas-mountain-race-countdown/)

**Justinas Leveika [ELITE]:** "Technically the Atlas Mountain Race is a gravel race… but you can be very fooled by that. Yeah, there is a lot of what most people recognise as 'gravel', but there is also a lot of very rocky and rough 'gravel' that most people would class as a rocky MTB trail! You want your tyre pressure to be hard enough so you won't puncture easily but not too hard, so you lose precious comfort. Most of the course is hard-packed gravel, so you want something easy rolling too. On my Trek hardtail, I ran 29×2.2" mountain bike tyres." (https://www.tailfin.cc/blog/10-lessons-learned-from-the-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Essential Kit:**

**Shona & Rich / Keep Pedalling [COMPETITIVE]:** "I always carry a couple of Voile straps. They are so useful for strapping things on the bike if something breaks, carrying an extra 1.5 litre bottle of coke strapped to a fork leg, or even attaching a tandem to the roof of a taxi! [...] Oh, a Backcountry Research Super 8 Strap. I always have one on the top tube for rapid access to my gloves or Mr Toms bars! Wolf Tooth Pack Pliers are a great tool to take and fit perfectly in a tool keg. And finally, an emergency bag of Skittles … Though I normally have to hide these so Rich doesn't eat them!" (https://advntr.cc/atlas-mountain-race-countdown/)

**Lighting:**

**James Mark Hayden [COMPETITIVE]:** "Very importantly a headtorch is highly recommended. A head torch has multiple uses: navigation when on foot, fixing bike, tough descents to see ahead and around turns, backup lighting if primary dies, lighting when going slow. As a battery light I use an Exposure Diablo, this light can be mounted easily both on the bars and on the helmet mount, it just clicks between them one handed." (https://jamesmarkhayden.eu/knowledge/tips-on-how-to-complete-the-atlas-mountain-race)

**Packing for Smaller Frames:**

**Mel Webb [COMPETITIVE]:** "Standing at 5'2, she's no stranger to the game of tetris that is packing a small bike." The article details extensive challenges fitting standard bikepacking bags on XS frames, including frame bags that bulged, handlebar bags that didn't fit between narrow bars, and saddle bags hitting tires. (https://theradavist.com/mel-webb-atlas-mountain-race)

## Terrain Details (Rider Perspective)

**First Night - Telouet Pass:**
**Michael Drummond [UNKNOWN]:** "Some sections proving rideable, almost enjoyable a short lived sensation as the feet again make connection with terra firma and the bike is used as a cumbersome hiking p[artner]" Describes the descent from the 2600m Telouet Pass as "Rocks, boulders and well, more boulders strewn along a convoluted and rarely trodden route that winds down the back of this peak enveloped in thick darkness." (https://advntr.cc/the-atlas-mountain-race-diary-pt-1-of-4/)

**Old Colonial Road:**
**Jochen Böhringer [ELITE]:** "One of the greatest sections of road was the Old Colonial Road - an incredible marvel of engineering. It has fallen into disrepair, with a little hike-a-bike required where two sections have fallen away. But I would have loved to have done it during the daytime to see the path improbably balancing on the mountainside." (https://ultra-jo.com/2022/12/03/amr-report/)

**Justinas Leveika [ELITE]:** "The so-called old colonial road seems to have gained an aura of magic, I had the pleasure to ride it in the dawn light and I can tell you it was amazing. Yes, it was rough with chunky rocks but I did not find it torrid. There were a few washed out sections that required crossing. I know some have said they were difficult, but I simply lowered my bike and climbed down, then hoisted it on my back and climbed back up." (https://www.tailfin.cc/blog/10-lessons-learned-from-the-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Riverbed Sections:**
**Kevin 'Benky' Benkenstein [ELITE]:** "There are, however, hours spent riding through dry river beds which consist of anything from sand and small stones to baby-head rocks that cover kilometres on end of the route. At times you are, quite simply, shaken." (https://escapecollective.com/bike-check-surviving-the-1-300-km-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Valerio Stuart [UNKNOWN]:** "The storm had rearranged everything. Sections of the trail had simply vanished. I waded through icy water more times than I could count. Progress was painfully slow." Describing the gorge section after flooding. (https://bikepacking.com/plog/riding-the-high-atlas-morocco/)

**Kings of the Atlas Section:**
**Valerio Stuart [UNKNOWN]:** "The trail led towards the Cathedral d'Imsfrane, an immense rock formation towering over a green valley. The 10-kilometre climb up to it felt endless, but the views made every slow pedal stroke worth it. The landscape looked at times like Utah—at least how I imagine it—and at times like Spain." (https://bikepacking.com/plog/riding-the-high-atlas-morocco/)

**Surface Composition:**
**James Mark Hayden [COMPETITIVE]:** "The terrain is off-road with minimal hike a bike and little really rough terrain, and almost no true mountain bike terrain - Ride with GPS says 43% paved and 57% unpaved." (https://jamesmarkhayden.eu/knowledge/tips-on-how-to-complete-the-atlas-mountain-race)

**Kevin 'Benky' Benkenstein [ELITE]:** "While it has a significant amount of paved roads, around 45% of the route is sealed, the non-sealed roads are mostly not 'gravel'. In general, the route is what I'd consider OG mountain bike terrain: jeep tracks, gravel roads of varying quality, walking paths, and fast rocky descents." (https://escapecollective.com/bike-check-surviving-the-1-300-km-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Sand Sections:**
**Michael Drummond [UNKNOWN]:** "A deep, fine and unstable medium that seemingly soaks up all exertion, a channel of sand that is all take. Billions of microscopic permutations of glass with an appetite for tyre and energy." Encountered after 20 hours in the saddle near the finish. (https://advntr.cc/the-atlas-mountain-race-diary-pt-1-of-4/)

**2026 Route Changes:**
Race organizers note: "For the first time since its inception we will start the race in Beni-Mellal, the capital of Beni-Mellal-Khénifra Region and a great jumping off point into the heart of the High Atlas. This allows us to explore a new crossing of the High Atlas and the incredible gorges of M'Goun. What this means is that the first 300km of the race route are completely new. It does also mean that we will no longer be heading over the iconic Telouet pass." Route increased to around 1350km and 25,000m of elevation. (https://www.themountainraces.cc/atlas-mountain-race)

**Valerio Stuart [UNKNOWN]:** "From the start in Beni-Mellal, riders head straight into the mountains. The first major resupply comes in Tabant at 180 km, after which the route climbs Tizi N'Aït Imi—the highest pass we've ever included in AMR at 2,910m—before dropping into the narrow, winding gorge of M'Goun. Conditions depending, riders will follow a mule trail through the river itself, with a diversion in place if needed due to weather conditions." (https://bikepacking.com/plog/riding-the-high-atlas-morocco/)

## Race Strategy & Pacing

**Sleep Strategy:**

**Rory Stuart [COMPETITIVE]:** "I stopped near-11pm most nights and had a quick bit of (second or third) dinner before sleep. Alarm set for 5-6am. Another bit of food and then back on the 'road'. [...] As a result, the urge to press snooze for more rest and to see more of the route in daylight was too great. I only rose after hearing the sound of freehubs whiz past." Finished in 6 days 4 hours 45 minutes but later reflected: "I wholeheartedly think I did pretty darn well. Sure, I could've slept less. But I had no major incidents (barring a near miss you'll read about later), my hands still function, and overall I had a pretty groovy time on a pretty epic event!" (https://kostume.cc/atlas-mountain-race-report-2025)

**Marei Moldenhauer [UNKNOWN]:** "I don't usually plan my sleep strategy in advance but rather decide on the go how much I need. This time, that approach didn't work so well. I ended up in a situation I never wanted to be in—I was on the bike but actually too tired to ride. That's something I never want to experience again. I've learned from this mistake, and for my next race, I'll plan potential sleep spots and times more carefully." After falling into dangerous fatigue, she slept one hour roadside: "that one hour made a huge difference. My brain was functioning again afterward." (https://cyclite.cc/en/blogs/news/atlas-mountain-race-marei-moldenhauer)

**James Mark Hayden [COMPETITIVE]:** "I decided to sleep outside every night in 2020, I am quite happy with this now and with the shorter duration I did not want to waste time if only sleeping 1h30 a night." (https://jamesmarkhayden.eu/knowledge/tips-on-how-to-complete-the-atlas-mountain-race)

**Jochen Böhringer [ELITE]:** "I continue my ride because I want to drive straight through the first night as I usually do during ultra-cycling races." After eventually stopping at a gas station due to flooding: "we make the best out of the situation by filling up our energy stores with some additional food, service our bikes and try to get some sleep on the floor of the shop while waiting for news that we can cross the river again." (https://ultra-jo.com/2022/12/03/amr-report/)

**Pacing and Time Management:**

**Justinas Leveika [ELITE]:** "Don't get fooled by 'just' 160 km to the next CP. The average speed in this race is very low. There is a lot of climbing involved, and the descents are not the type where you can relax. During most descents, you have to keep your concentration and can't just fly down as you might on a normal trail ride. So when planning the route and resupply locations take a close look at the elevation profile and type of surface you'll be riding on." (https://www.tailfin.cc/blog/10-lessons-learned-from-the-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Seb Breuer [ELITE]:** "Keep pedalling. Even if you are slow, keep moving and don't stop for too long. Stopping for too long would finally destroy your rhythm completely, making it far too difficult to start again." (https://www.tailfin.cc/blog/10-lessons-learned-from-the-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Shona & Rich / Keep Pedalling [COMPETITIVE]:** "First and foremost, enjoy it! Then take it a day at a time. If you aren't knocking out the big KM's you thought you'd do, that doesn't matter, what's important is just to keep going. Don't get disheartened if you feel your progress is slow- chances are everyone is feeling the same. On the first edition of the SRMR we were all about 3 days slower than we thought we'd be, including the winner Jay Petervary." (https://advntr.cc/atlas-mountain-race-countdown/)

**Checkpoint Strategy:**

**Justinas Leveika [ELITE]:** "Take your time at checkpoints. The mandatory checkpoints during the Atlas Mountain Race are a very safe bet to get everything you need. Providing plenty of food, a safe place to sleep, and even a shower. The problem with AMR is that the distance between the checkpoints can sometimes be pretty huge – It can be more than one day from one to the next and with no guarantee of resupply stops being open in between (depending on what time of day you ride through)." (https://www.tailfin.cc/blog/10-lessons-learned-from-the-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Jochen Böhringer [ELITE]:** "I finally arrive at the 3rd checkpoint in a village at around 650km grouping together again with Marin and Philippe for lunch while Justinas has already left defending his lead after taking a short nap and cleaning his wounds caused by some crashes. Some of us opt for a quick shower and I also do so to improve hygienic situation especially at my butt again. Considering that the high temperatures with lots of sweat and dust start to take its toll and I feel some saddle sores already." (https://ultra-jo.com/2022/12/03/amr-report/)

**Nutrition:**

**Marei Moldenhauer [UNKNOWN]:** "I ate a lot of omelets and tajine, but neither of those provides much energy. So, I ended up eating a lot of plain sugar, dates, or honey. But it got increasingly difficult. I had moments where I just couldn't eat anymore. It wasn't even that I ran out of food—I simply forgot to eat or didn't want to. At one point, I realized I had only eaten two Snickers bars and a few dates all day. That was way too little, but I had just lost my appetite." Note: Marei has celiac disease which complicated food options. (https://cyclite.cc/en/blogs/news/atlas-mountain-race-marei-moldenhauer)

**Justinas Leveika [ELITE]:** "Mint tea is your new Coke. Just like omelettes, mint tea is served everywhere and with a lot of sugar. That gives you kinda the same kick as Coke would. And warms you up too." Also noted: "The pastry game is very weak in Morocco. Yes, they have plain bread, which is served with everything, but that's about it. Don't expect muffins, croissants or other goodies at shops; sadly, most of the snacks there are very low on calories. The good news is yoghurt, high in protein and fats, can be found everywhere, just like the mint tea." (https://www.tailfin.cc/blog/10-lessons-learned-from-the-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Water Carrying:**

**James Mark Hayden [COMPETITIVE]:** "Water can be hard to come across in Morocco. Marking the resupply points and keeping them to hand for reference is something I find useful. Do not expect to find much – or any - water outside of these resupplies and if you do, consider it a bonus – a filter will be required for any non-bottled water. This lack of water means a large water carrying capacity will be needed. [...] I had a capacity of 5L and used it fully at time." (https://jamesmarkhayden.eu/knowledge/tips-on-how-to-complete-the-atlas-mountain-race)

**Marei Moldenhauer [UNKNOWN]:** "I knew I needed plenty of space for water, as there were long stretches without resupply points. This time, I carried an extra vest on my back for additional water because my frame bag left no room for bottles. With this setup, I had a total water capacity of 4.5 liters. [...] The longest stretch without water was around 100 km, with lots of hike-a-bike sections, so carrying enough was crucial." (https://cyclite.cc/en/blogs/news/atlas-mountain-race-marei-moldenhauer)

**Training:**

**Marei Moldenhauer [UNKNOWN]:** "It varied a lot due to my shift work. When I had weekend shifts and extra work during the week, I sometimes only managed 8 or 9 hours. In weeks with more free time, I reached up to 23 hours. I don't have a fixed average, but I'd estimate around 12 hours per week. So, a lot, but nothing extreme." She worked with coach Max Kinzelbauer, and noted: "If work gets particularly stressful, he adjusts my training accordingly. Because stress is stress, whether it's physical or mental." (https://cyclite.cc/en/blogs/news/atlas-mountain-race-marei-moldenhauer)

## DNF Risk Factors

**Weather-Related Failures:**

**Valerio Stuart [UNKNOWN]:** Described attempting to continue in extreme weather: "Part of me was disappointed at once again taking a step back, but another part of me knew that pushing into a huge storm on a remote 3,000-metre pass wasn't bold. It was stupid." He referenced scratching from BearBones 300 earlier in the year due to a huge storm. (https://bikepacking.com/plog/riding-the-high-atlas-morocco/)

**Jochen Böhringer [ELITE]:** "Justinas unfortunately had a bad crash in one of the downhill sections and had to scratch from the race" while he was in first position. (https://ultra-jo.com/2022/12/03/amr-report/)

**Sleep Deprivation Dangers:**

**Marei Moldenhauer [UNKNOWN]:** "I ended up in a situation I never wanted to be in—I was on the bike but actually too tired to ride. That's something I never want to experience again." She noticed it happening while riding uphill, which she noted made it "less dangerous than it could have been." (https://cyclite.cc/en/blogs/news/atlas-mountain-race-marei-moldenhauer)

**Mechanical/Equipment Issues:**

**Michael Drummond [UNKNOWN]:** "The first few hours felt like an endless process of discovery, settling into what was comfortable, thoroughly exploring all the sounds coming from my bike and stopping far too much in order to make inadequate diagnosis and less than professional attempts to repair things." (https://advntr.cc/the-atlas-mountain-race-diary-pt-1-of-4/)

**Scratch Rate:**

**Justinas Leveika [ELITE]:** "This race is not rider friendly. The so-called scratch rate is big. This is why it's so rewarding to have your name amongst the finishers." (https://www.tailfin.cc/blog/10-lessons-learned-from-the-atlas-mountain-race/)

**Water Management Failures:**

**James Mark Hayden [COMPETITIVE]:** "Trying to ride without water will at best slow you down, and at worst kill you – it's not one to take lightly." (https://jamesmarkhayden.eu/knowledge/tips-on-how-to-complete-the-atlas-mountain-race)

**Flooding/River Crossings:**

**Jochen Böhringer [ELITE]:** Described the race being temporarily stopped due to a flooded river crossing: "the heavy rain dramatically increased the water level of a close-by river to a level that we cannot cross it with our bikes anymore." Race director Nelson Trees had to personally verify safe crossing conditions before allowing racers to continue. (https://ultra-jo.com/2022/12/03/amr-report/)
