**Race Type:** Ultra-endurance bikepacking / Self-supported ITT
**Location:** Colorado Rocky Mountains, USA

## OFFICIAL DATA

### Race Overview
- **Official Name:** Colorado Trail Race (CTR)
- **Distance:** 527-540 miles (varies by source; official CTR route ~530 miles including wilderness detours)
- **Elevation Gain:** 70,000-75,000+ feet
- **Elevation Range:** 5,500 ft to 13,271 ft (high point near Coney Summit in San Juan Mountains)
- **Average Elevation:** 10,000+ feet
- **Singletrack:** 300+ miles (~70% of route)
- **Terrain:** Singletrack, jeep roads, gravel roads, significant hike-a-bike sections

### 2026 Race Details
- **Date:** August 2, 2026
- **Start Location:** Durango, CO (College Cafe, 128 E 9th St)

- **Entry Fee:** $50 mandatory donation to Colorado Trail Foundation

- **Format:** Self-supported, self-timed ITT (Individual Time Trial)

## TERRAIN & COURSE

### Trail Overview
- 486-mile official Colorado Trail (hiking route)
- ~530-540 miles for bike route with wilderness detours
- 33 trail segments
- Passes through 8 mountain ranges, 6 National Forests, 5 major river systems
- Continental Divide Trail co-located for ~253 miles (starting Segment 6)

### Elevation Profile
- Low point: ~5,500 ft (Denver/Waterton Canyon end)
- High point: 13,271 ft (slopes below Coney Summit, San Juan Mountains, end of Segment 22)
- Average: 10,000+ feet
- Total gain/loss: ~82,000-89,000 feet (varies by direction)
- Average: ~200 feet gain per mile

### Wilderness Areas & Detours (Bikes Prohibited)
Six wilderness areas require detours, adding ~46 miles to route:

1. **Lost Creek Wilderness** - ~70 mile road detour (replaces 24 miles of trail)
2. **Holy Cross Wilderness**
3. **Mt. Massive Wilderness**
4. **Collegiate Peaks Wilderness** - ~27 mile detour
5. **La Garita Wilderness** - ~56 mile detour
6. **Weminuche Wilderness** - ~18 mile detour

### Collegiate Split (East vs West)
Route splits into two options for ~80 miles (miles 180-260 from Denver):

**Collegiate East:**
- 78 miles, 17,800 ft elevation gain
- More gradual, mainly below treeline
- Better town access (Buena Vista, Mt. Princeton Hot Springs)
- More established bike detour through Buena Vista
- Access to 10 fourteeners
- Earlier season accessibility (typically hikeable in June)

**Collegiate West:**
- 83 miles, 19,800 ft elevation gain
- More rugged, scenic, exposed
- 15-mile section between 12,000-13,000 ft
- Hope Pass: 12,548 ft (2,600 ft gain in 2.5 miles)
- More hike-a-bike
- Co-located with CDT
- Later season only due to snow
- Limited bike-legal sections (CW01, CW04, CW05 only)

### Key Sections by Difficulty

**Early Sections (Denver to Breckenridge):**
- Generally fast, buff singletrack
- Exception: Rocky, rooty descent off Georgia Pass

**Ten Mile Range (After Highway 9 to Copper Mountain):**
- 12 miles of brutal, bumpy, rocky terrain
- Second rockiest section of entire trail

**Frisco to Twin Lakes (75 miles):**
- Trail becomes more difficult
- More rugged terrain heading west into Rockies

**Spring Creek Pass to Stony Pass Road:**
- ~30 consecutive miles above timberline
- Maximum exposure to weather

**San Juan Mountains (Silverton area):**
- Most hike-a-bike sections of entire CT
- High alpine traverse
- Beautiful but challenging
- Most variable weather
- Numerous hike-a-bikes of varying lengths

### Technical Difficulty Rating
- **Difficulty:** 9/10 (one of the harder long-distance bikepacking routes)
- **Rideability:** ~90%+ rideable (by time, not mileage)
- **Terrain types:** Rocky/rooty technical climbs and descents, flowy singletrack, gravel roads, "babyhead" strewn descents, staircase-like descents, talus sections (some nearly unrideable)

### Hike-a-Bike Reality
- "Hike-a-bikes are part and parcel of the Colorado Trail experience"
- Even racers have significant hike-a-bike on both uphills AND downhills
- High point of 13,271 ft followed by "20+ miles of tundra-talus-shale"
- Weight distribution critical: don't overload front end (constant lifting over rocks)

## WEATHER

### Timing
- Race typically late July or first week of August
- Trail usually snow-free by early-to-mid July
- August considered best month overall
- Timing balances snowmelt vs. monsoon season

### Conditions Overview
- "Rocky Mountain weather can be intense and change very quickly"
- Temperature range: Below freezing to 80s+ F (20-90 F range)
- Possible conditions ANY time: sunburn, thunderstorms, hail, snow, sleet, rain, fog, high winds

### Afternoon Thunderstorms
- Daily occurrence in July and August
- Lightning is serious, life-threatening danger at elevation
- Plan to be OFF high ridges and exposed areas by NOON
- San Juan Mountains especially notorious for violent afternoon storms
- Storms typically hit early afternoon, can last past dark

### Key Weather Hazards
1. **Lightning** - Primary danger at altitude
2. **Hypothermia** - Possible even in summer
3. **Dehydration** - High altitude, exertion, sun exposure
4. **Altitude sickness** - Major concern (see below)

### Altitude-Related Weather Concerns
- Most trail above 9,000 ft
- Thinner air = harder cardiovascular work
- Cold temperatures at night, especially at high passes
- Second half more exposed with longer above-treeline sections

### Monsoon Season
- July-August = monsoon season in Colorado
- Pace often dictated by afternoon storm timing
- Most variable weather in second half (Spring Creek to Durango)
- Long sections of above-treeline exposure increase risk

## ALTITUDE & HEALTH

### Altitude Sickness (AMS)
**Symptoms - Mild:**
- Headaches
- Fatigue
- Loss of appetite
- Nausea
- Dizziness

**Symptoms - Severe (EMERGENCY):**
- Lung congestion (HAPE - High Altitude Pulmonary Edema)
- Confusion (HACE - High Altitude Cerebral Edema)
- Loss of coordination
- **ACTION:** Descend immediately, potentially fatal

### Acclimatization Recommendations
- Spend 10 days prior to CTR at elevation (ideal)
- Minimum 2-3 days at altitude before starting
- 2+ weeks needed for optimal acclimatization
- Full acclimatization takes 6 weeks
- Arrive early OR right before race (avoid 5-7 day "dip")
- 90% adjustment takes ~3 weeks

### Physiological Effects
- Heart and lungs work harder at altitude
- Muscle stiffening possible
- 11.3% aerobic performance decrease within 1-3 hours of arrival
- Half of performance (5.7%) recovered after ~45 hours

### Day 1 Challenge (Durango Start)
- Going from ~7,000 ft (Durango) to 12,000 ft (Taylor Lake) on Day 1
- Hike-a-bike at 12,500+ ft in early days where altitude really hits

## FORUM INSIGHTS & COMMUNITY WISDOM

### Gear Philosophy
- "Go Light" - prioritize lighter gear due to 70-80K ft of vertical
- Ultralight approach common among successful finishers
- Alcohol stoves, lightweight bivys, tarps preferred
- 30-degree bag minimum (can be lighter)

### Bag Setup Recommendations
- Frame bag (MOST weight here)
- Handlebar bag
- Seat bag (less weight - leverage causes swinging)
- Feedbags for quick access
- Light daypack for snacks/water only
- Popular brand: Revelate Designs

### Bike Setup
- Hardtails work fine
- Full suspension common among racers
- Popular tires: Maxxis Ardent Race, Maxxis Minions, Maxxis Reckon Race
- Tire size: 2.35-2.4"
- Tubeless setup standard (carry 2 tubes, patch kit)
- 60 TPI tires to prevent flats
- Dropper post helpful for technical descents

### Pedals & Shoes Debate
- Flat pedals make frequent on/off easier (tons of hike-a-bike)
- Flat shoes FAR superior for hiking vs clipless
- Popular flats: Race Face Chester
- Popular flat shoes: Specialized Rime Flat
- If clipless: PEARL iZUMi X-Alp Summit (good hiking sole)

### Sleep System
- Tarp + bivy often preferred over tent (faster setup/teardown, lighter, smaller)
- Big Agnes Fly Creek 1P popular tent option
- Enlightened Equipment quilts
- Air pad for insulation
- Cold-rated sleeping bag for high-altitude nights

### Clothing Layers
- Base layer system
- Puffy jacket (down recommended)
- Rain shell (essential)
- Cheap rain pants for warmth/wind protection
- Sun hoodie recommended (intense Colorado sun)
- Prepare for 20-90 F temperature range

### Hydration
- Hydration vest (Salomon Adv Skin 5 popular)
- 1.5-2L capacity minimum
- Fork-mounted bottles option (frees frame space)
- 2L Platy Bottle for extra capacity
- Water generally available daily (few long dry stretches)
- Notable dry sections: Cochetopa Valley, Indian Trail Ridge

### Food Reality
"You will not be able to eat enough food on the trail"
- In towns: multiple meals back-to-back common
- Constant struggle to eat enough while riding
- Stomach issues common (Miron Golfman had issues from start of 2023 race)

### Navigation
- Garmin Edge 1030+, 1040 Solar popular
- Wahoo ELEMNT ROAM used by Morton
- Portable charger essential (phone, computer, lights)

### Physical Challenges
- Saddle sores: "By far, the most significant source of pain" from 12+ hours daily friction
- Hands/wrists fatigue from rough terrain
- Feet issues from hike-a-bike
- GI problems common

## BLACK PILL (Harsh Realities)

### DNF Rate
- **Average finish rate: ~25%**
- **Average DNF rate: ~75%**
- Three-quarters of starters do not finish
- One of highest DNF rates of any ultra-endurance event
- Comparison: Badwater 135 (~40% DNF), Leadville 100 (~56% DNF)

### Why People DNF
1. **Altitude sickness** - Hits hard and early
2. **Weather** - Storms, lightning, hypothermia
3. **Mechanical failures** - Remote sections, no support
4. **GI issues** - Stomach problems, inability to eat
5. **Physical breakdown** - Saddle sores, injury
6. **Mental fatigue** - Relentless difficulty
7. **Time/pacing issues** - Underestimating difficulty

### Course Brutality
- "The trail is not technical but relentless"
- High altitude defines the challenge
- Distance alone would be manageable; combination with altitude is brutal
- "Punishing twenty+ miles of tundra-talus-shale" after high point
- Weather danger is real: "Nothing else on the trail is as dangerous as a high altitude thunderstorm"

### Self-Rescue Reality
"It cannot be stressed enough that you are ON YOUR OWN"
- Seriously remote sections
- No cell service in many areas
- Medical assistance may be far, far away
- Self-evacuation likely only option

### Time Underestimation
- Biking trail takes 4-20 days depending on ability
- Record holders finish in ~4 days
- Most finishers: 6-10+ days
- Many attempt with insufficient fitness/preparation

### What the Race Really Is
"This is a hiking trail that bikes are allowed on, not a mountain bike-specific trail"
- Difficult singletrack sections
- Steep descents
- Epic hike-a-bikes "hard even to push a bike up"

### 2023 Race Reality Check
- 68 starters, only 19 finishers
- ~72% DNF rate that year
- Conditions, altitude, and difficulty take heavy toll

## LOGISTICS

### Getting to Start (Durango)
- Durango-La Plata County Airport (DRO)
- Nearest major airport: Albuquerque (~4 hours), Denver (~6 hours)
- College Cafe: 128 E 9th St, Durango
- 4:00 AM start time

### Finish (Denver/Waterton Canyon)
- Waterton Canyon Trailhead: 11300 Waterton Road, Littleton, CO 80125
- Southwest of Denver metro area
- Arrange transportation in advance

### Transportation Between Termini
- Greyhound bus service
- Private shuttles
- Rental car (one-way)
- Friend/family pickup
- ~6-7 hour drive between Denver and Durango

### Resupply Towns (South to North / Durango to Denver)

**Durango to Silverton area:**
- Silverton (limited services)

**Silverton to Creede:**
- Lake City (via Spring Creek Pass)
- Ravens Rest Hostel (former CDT thru-hiker run)
- Creede ("best resupply town on entire trail")

**Creede to Salida area:**
- No major towns for ~100 miles
- Monarch Mountain Lodge (limited services)
- Salida (~15 miles from road crossing)

**Buena Vista area:**
- Mt. Princeton Hot Springs Resort
- Buena Vista

**Twin Lakes to Leadville:**
- Twin Lakes Village (General Store - expensive, holds packages)
- Leadville (9 miles from trailhead, Safeway, Inn the Clouds Hostel)

**Copper Mountain to Frisco/Breckenridge:**
- Copper Mountain (convenience store, not a town)
- Frisco (Safeway, Whole Foods, Walmart, laundromat)
- Breckenridge
- Free Summit County shuttle between towns

**North of Breckenridge:**
- Jefferson (via Kenosha Pass)
- Fairplay (via Kenosha Pass, further but more amenities)
- Bailey (full grocery store, mile 41)

### Long Stretches Without Resupply
- Silverton to Mt. Princeton Hot Springs: ~181 miles (only one resupply option)
- Frisco to Twin Lakes: 75 miles
- Various 50+ mile stretches

### Lodging Options
- Inn the Clouds Hostel (Leadville) - hiker/biker friendly
- Ravens Rest (Lake City) - run by former CDT thru-hiker
- Monarch Mountain Lodge
- Various hotels in larger towns

### Package Drops
- No post office in Twin Lakes (General Store holds packages)
- General delivery available in towns with post offices
- CT Databook has zip codes and mile markers for resupply

### Pre-Race Preparation
- 10 days altitude acclimatization recommended
- Arrive in Colorado early
- Stay at intermediate altitude (Denver ~5,280 ft) before race

## TRIPLE CROWN CHALLENGE

### Overview
Completing three classic bikepacking races in one calendar year:
1. **Tour Divide** (June) - ~2,745 miles, Canada to Mexico
2. **Colorado Trail Race** (August) - ~530 miles
3. **Arizona Trail Race** (October) - ~800 miles

### Challenge Significance
- CTR is the middle event of the Triple Crown
- Created by late David Goldberg in 2012

## GRAVEL GOD RATINGS

### Overall Difficulty: 9.5/10
The Colorado Trail Race is among the most difficult bikepacking events in the world. The combination of 500+ miles, 70,000+ feet of climbing, sustained high altitude (averaging 10,000+ feet), extreme weather variability, technical terrain, and self-supported format creates an extraordinarily challenging event.

### Category Breakdown

**Distance:** 9/10
- 530+ miles is a massive undertaking
- Comparable to Tour Divide daily mileage but condensed
- Requires sustained multi-day effort

**Elevation/Climbing:** 10/10
- 70,000-75,000 feet of climbing
- Relentless up and down
- Average 200 ft gain per mile
- No flat sections for recovery

**Altitude:** 10/10
- Average 10,000+ feet
- Maximum 13,271 feet
- 30+ consecutive miles above timberline
- Altitude sickness is real threat
- Significant aerobic penalty

**Technical Difficulty:** 8/10
- 300+ miles of singletrack
- Rocky, rooty sections
- Significant hike-a-bike (even downhill)
- Talus, babyhead rocks, staircase descents
- Not the most technical, but relentless

**Weather Risk:** 9/10
- Daily afternoon thunderstorms in season
- Lightning danger at altitude
- Temperature swings 20-90 F
- Snow, hail, hypothermia possible
- Monsoon season timing

**Remoteness/Self-Sufficiency:** 9/10
- Self-rescue often only option
- Limited cell service
- Long stretches between resupply
- No support, no aid stations
- Must carry/source all needs

**Navigation:** 6/10
- Well-established route
- GPX files available
- Trail generally marked
- Wilderness detours require attention
- Not a significant challenge for prepared riders

**Competition Level:** 8/10
- Top finishers include professional-level athletes
- Lachlan Morton (WorldTour pro) held record
- Strong ultra-endurance community
- No prizes but prestige significant

**Accessibility/Entry Barrier:** 7/10
- No official registration
- Limited to 74 Grand Depart spots
- $50 donation required
- ITT option available anytime
- Geographic/travel barrier for non-US riders

**Overall "Type 2 Fun" Factor:** 10/10
- 75% DNF rate speaks for itself
- "Miserable but transformative" experience
- Extreme physical and mental challenge
- Finishing is a significant life achievement

### Comparison to Other Events
- **Harder than:** Most gravel races, Unbound 200, BWR
- **Similar to:** Arizona Trail Race, other long ITT events
- **Slightly easier than:** Tour Divide (by distance)
- **Different challenge than:** Road stage races (altitude/terrain vs pure watts)

### Who Should Attempt
- Experienced ultra-endurance cyclists
- Strong mountain bikers comfortable with technical terrain
- Those who have completed 12+ hour events
- Riders with altitude experience or time to acclimatize
- Mentally resilient individuals prepared for suffering
- Those with solid bikepacking/self-sufficiency skills

### Who Should NOT Attempt (Yet)
- First-time bikepackers
- Those unfamiliar with high altitude
- Riders without significant hike-a-bike experience
- Anyone expecting a "gravel race" experience
- Those unable to commit to serious training and preparation
