# ICEMAN COMETH — COMMUNITY RESEARCH

## Rider Quotes & Race Reports

**Kedzie Ruckle [COMPETITIVE]:** "This year's Iceman felt like the first time everything really clicked for me. From the moment I rolled into the start chute, I could tell the training I put in all season with MISCA Devo and my coach, Sara Vano, had paid off. I felt stronger, smoother, and more confident than any Iceman before." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Kedzie Ruckle [COMPETITIVE]:** "The community this year felt unmatched; everywhere I looked, someone was cheering, encouraging, or suffering right alongside me. Iceman is always known for its community, but this year felt on a whole different level But… it wouldn't be Iceman without some chaos… Once we caught the last adult wave before Sows Ear, everything backed up fast. The trail went from smooth racing to full-on gridlock. There were sections where the line was completely stopped, and honestly, there were moments where I'm pretty sure I could've carried my bike through the woods faster than waiting out some of those jams." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Kedzie Ruckle [COMPETITIVE]:** "I really hope next year there's more of a gap between waves or a different way of managing the traffic. The congestion definitely slowed down a lot of riders who were ready to flow." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Kedzie Ruckle [COMPETITIVE]:** "The increase in singletrack made everything more exciting, more technical, and more fun. It played to the strengths I've been building all season, especially with how much Devo has helped me improve bike handling and efficiency. When I had open trail, I felt fast and controlled, which is a huge confidence boost heading into next season." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Anonymous Competitive Rider [COMPETITIVE]:** "Around May, I went in for my annual check-up and was put on a new medication. By July, I had a significant reaction to it and lost over 60 lbs... My FTP dropped from 280 to 215. I couldn't finish a regular ride without bonking." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Anonymous Competitive Rider [COMPETITIVE]:** "Sow's Ear wasn't nearly as bad as the comments made it out to be. It felt like riding moguls, but it was completely rideable, just something to get through. The two-track afterwards helped spread riders out, which was huge." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Anonymous Competitive Rider [COMPETITIVE]:** "I wasn't excited about climbing Icebreaker straight into Woodchip, but the new Red Bull corner that sent us back down was actually awesome. What wasn't great was having zero momentum going into Woodchip." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Anonymous Competitive Rider [COMPETITIVE] on race strategy:** "I had my goals written on painter's tape: 12.5 mph avg, Dockery by 36:00, Williamsburg by 1:36. If I can stick to that, I'd have a shot at sub-2:30." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Anonymous Competitive Rider [COMPETITIVE]:** "When we took off, I sprinted hard to catch up with a group. Facing that headwind, I didn't care about burning a match or hitting a PR – I needed a draft. I tucked in behind the third wheel and let the group pull me along." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Anonymous Competitive Rider [COMPETITIVE]:** "Once we crested Williamsburg, something clicked. I felt amazing. No fatigue. No cramps. Not a single sign of exhaustion. even cold. At that moment, I knew all the training had paid off." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Rick DeShields [COMPETITIVE]:** "While the race is not technical it can be hard simply because you have to push your limits the entire race. From the start to the finish everyone is going 95% and saves the last 10% for the last mile just to show off a little. Yes, my math is correct!" (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Rick DeShields [COMPETITIVE]:** "I caught up to a tight group of racers at one of the few hills on the first half of the course and a couple of racers from the pack spun out and stopped which forced the chasers including myself to dismount and run up the climb. At the top I did a running jump back onto the bike and took off." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Rick DeShields [COMPETITIVE]:** "The descents were fast. So fast that in one descent I pulled a two-wheel slide before hitting 2 rollers that I managed to pull a gap jump on. I let out a big 'hell yeah' but the racer behind me was not nearly as enthused as I was over my line and said that he would not be following my line anymore." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Rick DeShields [COMPETITIVE]:** "The first was Anita's climb which starts as a slowly ascending climb to slow you down before shooting up a lose sandy wall followed by a false flat that took us up even more. The key here is to pace yourself because shortly afterwards the course takes you back down and then right back up another gradual hill." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Rick DeShields [COMPETITIVE]:** "About a mile or so from the finish the course sends you up 2 hills. The last, called 'Icebreaker' hill is harder than it looks. It's a long gradual climb that slowly shoots up as it reaches the top. Of course they put a video camera and a photographer on the hill when you look and feel your worst!" (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Rick DeShields [COMPETITIVE]:** Finished in 2:39. His teammates: Jamie finished in 2:22, Brian and Mark finished in 3:12 and 3:13 respectively. (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Michael Green [ELITE]:** "The last climb isn't particularly steep, it isn't particularly long but after 30 miles and being close to the red line, it just sucks enough. ## Terrain Details (Rider Perspective)

**Course Overview [COMPETITIVE]:** "The course is mostly sandy doubletrack with a few sections of singletrack thrown in for fun. The trails start relatively flat with a few small hills but around mile 24 they start throwing some big hills and hard climbs at you to wear you down before you finish." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Sow's Ear Section [COMPETITIVE]:** "Sow's Ear wasn't nearly as bad as the comments made it out to be. It felt like riding moguls, but it was completely rideable, just something to get through. The two-track afterwards helped spread riders out, which was huge." Speed through Sow's Ear during pre-ride: 9.6 mph; race day: 8.5 mph. (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Gussied Up Section [COMPETITIVE]:** "As we approached the new Gussied Up section, I felt incredible. The best singletrack ride I've ever had. Legs felt strong, confidence was high. Then we slowed down… and stopped. No crash, no help needed, just a long bottleneck. Everyone was wondering why we had come to a halt. Once we saw the hill, people said, 'Really? This is what's holding us up?'" (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Valley Section [COMPETITIVE]:** "After mile 20 the course starts to go down into a valley with a few mid-size climbs to make things fun. The descents were fast." Followed by "about a mile of flat windy trail before the climbs out started." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Anita's Climb [COMPETITIVE]:** "The first was Anita's climb which starts as a slowly ascending climb to slow you down before shooting up a lose sandy wall followed by a false flat that took us up even more." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Icebreaker Hill [COMPETITIVE]:** "About a mile or so from the finish the course sends you up 2 hills. The last, called 'Icebreaker' hill is harder than it looks. It's a long gradual climb that slowly shoots up as it reaches the top." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Course Changes [COMPETITIVE]:** "I wasn't excited about climbing Icebreaker straight into Woodchip, but the new Red Bull corner that sent us back down was actually awesome. What wasn't great was having zero momentum going into Woodchip." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Finish Section [ELITE]:** "As you enter the finish chute there is an off-camber left hand turn and then some fresh cut trail which dives down into a pit and through a half buried shipping container. After the container the trail heads into the celebration zone where everyone is watching the racers finish... Just before the finish there was a short steep 3 foot drop before the final turn to the finish line." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Course Changes [COMPETITIVE]:** "This years track was heavy with singletrack and a little less climbing." (https://www.bikeman.com/blog/iceman-cometh-2024.htms)

**Start Section [COMPETITIVE]:** "Singletrack about 2 miles into the 30 mile day." (https://www.bikeman.com/blog/iceman-cometh-2024.htms)

## Race Strategy & Pacing

**Anonymous Competitive Rider [COMPETITIVE] - Time Goals:** "I had my goals written on painter's tape: 12.5 mph avg, Dockery by 36:00, Williamsburg by 1:36. If I can stick to that, I'd have a shot at sub-2:30." Reached Dockery just after 37 minutes. After traffic delays: "I knew PR hopes, sub-2:30 hopes, even sub-2:20 hopes were gone. But my race wasn't. I hammered. I passed when safe. I PR'd Make It Stick by 10 seconds." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Anonymous Competitive Rider [COMPETITIVE] - Draft Strategy:** "When we took off, I sprinted hard to catch up with a group. Facing that headwind, I didn't care about burning a match or hitting a PR – I needed a draft. I tucked in behind the third wheel and let the group pull me along. I recorded my second-fastest time out of the airport and set a PR on the segment to the first singletrack." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Rick DeShields [COMPETITIVE] - Pacing Strategy:** "Some racers went out fast, but my plan was to start a little conservative and get warmed up for a few miles while the wave spread out on the course. Once I got warmed up I sped up and settled into my race pace. I started passing other racers and making my way up the field and was even starting to pass racers from earlier waves." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Michael Green [ELITE] - Start Strategy:** "Standing in the corral awaiting the start of Wave 1, I decided to shed some layers in a panicked last minute change. Just a wool base-layer and my Bikeman long sleeve jersey." (https://www.bikeman.com/blog/iceman-cometh-2024.htms)

**Alexey Vermeulen [ELITE] - Race Without Power:** "This year, unlike '18, '19 and '21, I had no plan, I wanted to race by gut feelings and instinct. No power meter, just speed and distance to go on my Wahoo - a reminder that the only thing that would tell me how I was feeling was ME!" (https://silca.cc/en-gb/blogs/silca/winning-iceman-cometh)

**Alexey Vermeulen [ELITE] - Tactical Move:** "As I decided I was going to make the move with 12km to go, I found myself on the front and said why not? The pace had lulled a bit, I figured most people were waiting for the finish and I wanted to take my chance." Made separation with about 5km to go. (https://silca.cc/en-gb/blogs/silca/winning-iceman-cometh)

**Alexey Vermeulen [ELITE] - Mental Focus:** "I use 'Let Op' as my constant in these moments - which means pay attention in Dutch (an ode to my Opa who got me into the sport). Pay attention to line choice, gearing, and above all how hard you are going at a given time - not all power files are equal even if time is!" (https://silca.cc/en-gb/blogs/silca/winning-iceman-cometh)

**Kedzie Ruckle [COMPETITIVE] - Group Riding:** "Riding with a group that's moving the same pace as you gives you energy you can't really explain until you're in it: the drafting, the shared effort, the subtle communication, and that unspoken agreement that we're all pulling each other forward. It felt like real racing." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Ryder Pankey (9 years old) [COMPETITIVE]:** "It was really just, like, steady, consistent pace, and at the end it was starting to get hard, but I just kept pushing." Finished in 2:49 at age 9. **General Strategy [COMPETITIVE]:** "While the race is not technical it can be hard simply because you have to push your limits the entire race. **Chris Mutnansky [COMPETITIVE] - Training Race Strategy:** "Treat this one [Peak 2 Peak] like a full-dress rehearsal for Iceman: wear what you plan to wear, fuel how you plan to fuel, and race it like it's Kalkaska to Timber Ridge." ## Community Feel & Atmosphere

**Community Atmosphere [COMPETITIVE]:** "The community this year felt unmatched; everywhere I looked, someone was cheering, encouraging, or suffering right alongside me. Iceman is always known for its community, but this year felt on a whole different level." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Finish Line Atmosphere [ELITE]:** "There are people along most of the entire 30 mile point to point course, but you first start to really hear the finish line noise around 3 miles out as you start to skirt the edge of Timber Ridge. As you ride closer and come into the bottom of Icebreaker though, the noise is DEFINING! The pain you feel seems further away as you finish the climb and head into the final single track, your ears ringing slightly. Before you know it you are turning onto Woodchip, the final climb, and once again you can't think, you just pedal! You crest the top and hear the announcer yelling and the barriers being whacked!" (https://silca.cc/en-gb/blogs/silca/winning-iceman-cometh)

**Event Scale:** "The 2010 Iceman Cometh Challenge saw close to 4,700 riders across three events — the Iceman 29.5-mile point-to-point, the 16.5-mile Slushcup and the Kid's SnoCone race." Registration "reached capacity in less than one day. 20 percent of the racers came from 37 states outside Michigan." Total prize purse of roughly $43,000. Pro winners walked away $3,500 each. (https://velo.outsideonline.com/road/road-culture/life-as-a-bike-jockey-why-is-the-iceman-so-hot/)

**Race Director Steve Brown:** "You'll have riders who train for this race and some who will pull their bike out of the garage two weeks before." (https://velo.outsideonline.com/road/road-culture/life-as-a-bike-jockey-why-is-the-iceman-so-hot/)

**Finish Line Setup:** "All events finish in the campgrounds of the Pere Marquette State Forest, making for crowd sizes to match the jumbotron results board. The gathering grows till the last event of the day with the finish of the men's and women's pro race; a deliberate design by Brown. '[People] are there celebrating their own finish and then sticking around to see their heroes ride' he said." (https://velo.outsideonline.com/road/road-culture/life-as-a-bike-jockey-why-is-the-iceman-so-hot/)

**Rick Kozole [COMPETITIVE]:** "What's nice about the race is it's a family sport. It's not just for hardcore racers." Came out with a crew of 15 friends and family. (https://velo.outsideonline.com/road/road-culture/life-as-a-bike-jockey-why-is-the-iceman-so-hot/)

**Kent Noller (Party Bus Owner):** "Never having raced the event himself, Noller has been to the Iceman about eight years now to watch the race, visit with friends and 'guilt himself into exercising and eating right by surrounding [himself] into with world class athletes.'" The bus "has hosted about six national mountain bike champs, a few Olympians and one World Champ for post-Iceman celebrations." (https://velo.outsideonline.com/road/road-culture/life-as-a-bike-jockey-why-is-the-iceman-so-hot/)

**Post-Race Scene [COMPETITIVE]:** "As you enter the finish chute there is an off-camber left hand turn and then some fresh cut trail which dives down into a pit and through a half buried shipping container. After the container the trail heads into the celebration zone where everyone is watching the racers finish." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Expo and Pre-Race [COMPETITIVE]:** "The expo is always a highlight, and watching the Bonk Bros do a live podcast was pretty cool. Hearing them talk about the race eased my nerves. Spending time with my wife and kid kept me grounded. The Iceman group text, where everyone was buzzing with last-minute prep, was the final touch of a year-long buildup." (https://blog.iceman.com/)

**Peak 2 Peak Atmosphere [COMPETITIVE]:** "Crystal Mountain in late October is pure Michigan fall colors glowing, riders swapping stories, beers flowing at the finish. It's the closest you'll get to Iceman energy until the big day itself." (https://blog.iceman.com/index.php/2025/08/21/countdown-to-iceman-events-to-test-your-legs/)

**Community Impact [Race Director]:** "Brown reports the race has an economic impact of roughly $1.5 million on Traverse City and the surrounding the area. Community groups look forward to participating in the Iceman. Local high school sport teams volunteer for the race and in return receive fund raising donations." (https://velo.outsideonline.com/road/road-culture/life-as-a-bike-jockey-why-is-the-iceman-so-hot/)

**Cultural Impact [Race Director]:** "Brown said he 'gets a kick out of seeing a cycling culture develop in Traverse City. There's a lot more commuting by bike.' Just this summer, Brown bumped into a father of one of the football team volunteers. The parent had lost about 50 pounds and looked a completely changed person. A new-found love of cycling, instigated by the Iceman, was the cause." (https://velo.outsideonline.com/road/road-culture/life-as-a-bike-jockey-why-is-the-iceman-so-hot/)

**Rick DeShields [COMPETITIVE]:** "Iceman is more like a big 5000 person group ride to the festival at the finish line which is a lot more fun when you have friends that come up with you." (https://camba.us/wp/archives/7200)

**Start Line Atmosphere [ELITE]:** "We have the start line dialed. Subaru is a major sponsor and if you drive a Subaru you get preferred parking, and that we did, front row watching the sun rise and my fellow racers warming up in the cold on the airport runway in Kalkaska, Michigan, the location for the start of this incredible race weekend." (https://www.bikeman.com/blog/iceman-cometh-2024.htms)

**National Anthem [ELITE]:** "The National Anthem was played by the local high school band and we were suddenly flying around the airport, fighting for position." (https://www.bikeman.com/blog/iceman-cometh-2024.htms)
