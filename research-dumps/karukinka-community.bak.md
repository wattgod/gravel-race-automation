## Race Logistics & Travel

**Getting there takes serious planning.** After a 13-hour flight to Santiago, riders need another 3+ hour flight to Punta Arenas, followed by an 8-hour minibus journey to Villa Cameron (the start). The journey includes crossing the Straits of Magellan by ferry to reach Tierra del Fuego island. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Accommodations are extremely basic.** The first night is spent in a communal gym in Villa Cameron (population: 90) on portable beds, requiring riders to bring their own sleeping bags. Subsequent nights involve sleeping in albergues (basic mountain lodges) or private houses lent to the organization. At Caleta María, riders shared a loft and rooms in just two houses - the only structures at that location. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Race capacity is limited by infrastructure.** At Caleta María, there was space for only 50-60 people including crew, which is why "this event will never be massive and only those 50 people per year will be the lucky ones who get to enjoy this unique experience." (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**What's included:** Transportation from Punta Arenas to Tierra del Fuego and back, bike/bag transport between stages, all meals (breakfasts, lunches, dinners), hydration/feed stations, albergue accommodation, official race jersey, timing, finisher medal, official photos, basic mechanical support, satellite phone communication, satellite internet, accident insurance, ambulance and paramedics on route, and support vehicles. Notable exclusions: flights to Punta Arenas, optional hostel/cabin upgrades, and personal camping gear (tent/sleeping bag if choosing the camping option). (https://karukinkagravelrace.com/)

## Weather Experienced

**Highly variable and unpredictable conditions.** One stage saw snow falling all night before Stage 2, forcing a one-hour start delay to allow warmer temperatures to melt the snow. The race director noted that "weather conditions can change rapidly in Tierra de Fuego and you need to be prepared for everything, it can get very cold and snow or it can get warm and sunny with big temperature and condition changes over very short periods of time." (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Wind is a major factor.** The Patagonian steppe features wind "which very often blows at over 100 km/h." Multiple riders mentioned battling strong headwinds throughout stages. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel, https://www.bhbikes.com/en_GB/stories/gravelxr/antonio-ortiz-karukinka-gravel-race)

**Austral summer daylight is extreme.** The December race dates feature days that "begin at 04 am and terminate at 23 hours" - giving riders exceptionally long daylight for stages. (https://karukinkagravelrace.com/)

## Terrain Details (Rider Perspective)

**The race takes place almost entirely on the Ruta Vicuña-Yendegaia (Y-85 road)**, a work-in-progress military road built by the Chilean Army's Cuerpo Militar del Trabajo (CMT). This is "the one and only one [road] on the area" - riders go out to the end of the road, then return the same way because there are no alternatives. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Riders were given exclusive access to unopened road sections.** The Chilean army opened approximately 10 km of newly built road that was not yet open to the public, making participants "the first ones to cycle on that road which went into deep valleys surrounded by glaciers." The army builds about 1 km of road per year. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Stage 1 (98 km, 800m ascent):** Started immediately with a nasty climb where attacks began "just one minute into the race." The stage crossed a steppe with no vegetation but significant wind. "The last 10 km of the stage were beautiful as we got into a forest with small trees and the track had many twists and turns making it more exciting to ride." Finished at Lago Blanco, a big lake at the foot of snow-capped mountains. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Stage 2 (114 km, ~2000m ascent) - "A hard mountain stage":** Two long climbs crossing a huge mountain range, described as "one of the most beautiful and magical places I have visited." The course featured "endless forests and peaks on a track full of hairpins" finishing at Caleta María on the Strait of Magellan - literally "the end of the road." (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**The landscape varies dramatically:** "alternates between the Magellanic pampa, with its first forests where a significant population of guanacos live, and the lake area" featuring Lynch Lake, Escondido Lake, Blanco Lake, and Fagnano Lake (Kami). (https://stageraces.com/event/karukinka-gravel-race/)

**Total route specs:** 440 km over 5 stages (note: some sources say 4 stages for 2024, 5 stages for 2025) with 5,500 meters of total climbing, progressing through "pampa, bosques autóctonos, lagos, ríos" and crossing "the two most austral mountain passes in the world." (https://karukinkagravelrace.com/)

## Community Feel & Atmosphere

**Communal living creates strong bonds.** From arrival, "all the race participants and the crew started to live as a community as we shared the meals and the space where we slept." Despite it being a competitive race, there were "no distinctions" between pro riders like Robert Gesink and Jetse Bol and other participants - they were all "part of the race community sharing with the rest of the participants." (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Elite riders participating as equals:** Robert Gesink (retired pro with major victories including 2012 Tour of California, 2011 Tour of Oman, 2010 GP Montreal) participated in the 2024 edition alongside Jetse Bol and Dennis van Winden. Gesink is described as having "transitioned to a life enjoying riding his bike with friends and travelling." (https://www.granfondoguide.com/Contents/PopUp/8187/robert-gesink-to-participate-in-the-karukinka-gravel-race-in-chile-)

**Local engagement adds cultural depth.** Before Stage 1, riders had "some protocol ceremony with the local authorities and spending some time with the kids from the village" in Villa Cameron. At Caleta María, Don Julio (the property owner, a cycling enthusiast and nearly 70 years old) shared stories around a campfire about pioneering cycling in the region and his love for the area. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Post-race celebration:** The event concludes with an "Asado de Premiación" (award barbecue) on the final night in Villa Cameron, featuring slow-cooked meat on lenga wood. (https://karukinkagravelrace.com/, https://www.bhbikes.com/en_GB/stories/gravelxr/antonio-ortiz-karukinka-gravel-race)

**Boutique scale is intentional:** The race is described as "an experience designed to enjoy the roads, landscapes and camaraderie of a competition designed to totally disconnect between your passion and nature." The organization explicitly states this event "has born to be a boutique small event for just the lucky ones." (https://www.granfondoguide.com/Contents/PopUp/8187/robert-gesink-to-participate-in-the-karukinka-gravel-race-in-chile-, https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

## Wildlife & Natural Features

**King Penguins made a surprise appearance** at Caleta María to greet riders - described as "not very common in this place" which made the encounter especially special. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Significant guanaco populations** inhabit the "llanura del río Grande" (Grande River plains) where pampa alternates with the first forests. (https://karukinkagravelrace.com/)

**The Straits of Magellan crossing** is a significant moment - riders traveled by ferry across waters discovered during the first circumnavigation by Magellan and Elcano. "We could feel our ferry was being moved by the waves of the choppy sea." (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Complete isolation from civilization.** Between villages, riders encountered "nothing. Nothing apart from amazing ocean views, pure nature and amazing landscapes with some wild animals. But no cars, no villages, no trucks, no coffee shops." At Caleta María, there are literally only two private houses - "No hotels, restaurants, shops, cafes, nothing." (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

## Race Format & Competition

**Racing starts immediately and hard [COMPETITIVE observation].** Despite the communal atmosphere and multi-day format, "As soon as we were outside Villa Cameron, which was just one minute into the race, the race mode was on! Straight away there was a nasty climb in which we could see the first attacks and the racing kicked off." (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**The race has an official timing system** (race result platform used) and finisher medals, but the exact format (GC time vs. stage racing) is not specified in rider reports. (https://my.raceresult.com/270903/results)

**Part of Gran Fondo World Tour Gravel Series:** The race is an official event in the 2024 Gran Fondo World Tour® GRAVEL series. (https://www.granfondoguide.com/Contents/PopUp/8187/robert-gesink-to-participate-in-the-karukinka-gravel-race-in-chile-)

**Race editions:** First edition ran December 6-9, 2023. Second edition December 4-7, 2024. Third edition scheduled for December 8-12, 2025 (5 stages). (https://my.raceresult.com/270903/results, https://karukinkagravelrace.com/)

## Historical & Cultural Context

**The island's name has origins in Magellan's voyage:** Tierra del Fuego (Land of Fire) was named by Magellan when "he could see from his ship many campfires which the locals used to warm up themselves during the region's cold weather." (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Indigenous heritage:** The island has been home to the Selk'nam, Haush, and Yamana indigenous communities "who have inhabited the region during thousands of years." (https://karukinkagravelrace.com/)

**Military road-building project:** The Ruta Vicuña-Yendegaia is being constructed by the Chilean Army "to show sovereignty of the land on this part of Tierra de Fuego" with the long-term goal to "connect by road and ferry with the most southern city in the world, Puerto Williams." Currently a work in progress at about 1 km per year construction pace. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Villa Cameron is tiny:** The start/finish village has just 90 inhabitants and one small shop - no restaurants or hotels. (https://www.gravelunion.cc/article/armchair-adventure-karukinka-gravel)

**Organizer experience:** The race is run by MTB Patagonia, which has "more than 15 years of experience in sporting events in the extreme south of Chile." (https://www.granfondoguide.com/Contents/PopUp/8187/robert-gesink-to-participate-in-the-karukinka-gravel-race-in-chile-)
