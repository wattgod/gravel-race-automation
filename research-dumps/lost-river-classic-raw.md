- Primarily gravel forest service roads
- Mix of hard-packed gravel and looser rocky sections
- Technical descents with embedded rocks
- Some singletrack crossings reported

- Constant rolling terrain through Appalachian foothills
- No single "mega-climb" but cumulative elevation via repeated climbs
- Technical descents requiring strong bike handling

- Miles 50-70: Remote backcountry with sustained climbing
- Miles 70-85: Final push with last major climb before descent to finish

## SUFFERING ZONES

**Zone 1: Mile 20-25 "North Mountain Approach"**
Steep, sustained climb on loose gravel. First real test of climbing legs. Loose surface punishes smooth cadence.

**Zone 2: Mile 40-45 "Technical Descent into Trout Run Valley"**
Rocky, rutted descent requiring full attention. Embedded rocks, off-camber turns. Crashes common here.

**Zone 3: Mile 55-62 "Remote Backcountry Climbs"**
Series of punchy climbs in remote forest sections. No bail-out options. Cumulative fatigue starts hitting hard.

**Zone 4: Mile 75-80 "Final Ascent"**
Last major climb when legs are cooked. Steep pitches on deteriorating surface. Mental battle to not walk.

## EQUIPMENT CONSENSUS

**Tires:**
- 40-45mm gravel tires (e.g., Specialized Pathfinder, Continental Terra Speed)
- Tubeless mandatory for rocky sections
- Some riders run 45-50mm for extra cushion and traction on technical descents

**Gearing:**
- 1x with 10-50 or 10-52 cassette highly recommended
- 2x with 34/32 or lower also viable
- West Virginia grades require genuine low gearing

**Frame/Protection:**
- Frame protection (tape or guards) for rocky descents
- Durable wheels over lightweight race wheels
- Dropper post not necessary but helpful on steep descents

**Hydration:**
- 3+ bottles or hydration pack + bottles
- Electrolyte supplementation critical in August heat
- Plan for ~20-24 oz per hour in humid conditions
