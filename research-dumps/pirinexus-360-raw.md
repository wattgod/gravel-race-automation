## 2. RACE HISTORY & FOUNDING

### Origin Story

**Founder:** Jordi Cantal (born 1971, Santa Coloma de Farners - 20km south of Girona)

**The Genesis (Summer 2014):**
> "When Jordi Cantal began to travel the Pirinexus route of 340 km in summer 2014, he did not know that at the end, deep in the night, he would have created a new challenge that has already become a legend."

Cantal completed the route alone in 18 hours during that first ride, and this personal milestone evolved into an organized event-challenge.

### Jordi Cantal Background
- **2008:** Started working as cycling guide for Bikecat (pioneering Girona cycling company)
- **2010:** Began organizing gravel tours with friend Michael Barry (ex-professional cyclist) - notably before "gravel" was even a mainstream term
- **2011:** Entered cycling world through professional road cycling connections
- **2014:** Created the Pirinexus 360 Challenge concept
- **2015:** Officially designed and directed "The Pirinexus Challenge 360" as a circular, trans-border endurance race
- **2017:** Began designing and directing gravel events for Sea Otter Europe festival
- **2019:** Founded the Girona Gravel Cycling Club

### Event Evolution
- **7 editions** held to date
- Now part of the Gran Fondo World Tour Gravel Series
- Part of UCI Gravel World Series at Sea Otter Europe
- Considered "one of the most authentic, beautiful, tough and prestigious gravel races in Europe"

### Bikepacking Heritage
The Pirinexus route itself predates the race - it was developed as a cycling-tourism route connecting the greenways (Vias Verdes) of the Girona region. The challenge format taps into the global bikepacking/ultra-endurance movement, combining:
- Self-supported riding ethos
- Checkpoint-based validation (route book stamps)
- Non-competitive "personal achievement" philosophy alongside racing
- The romance of finishing under the stars

## 3. TERRAIN & COURSE

### Route Overview

The Pirinexus is a 340-kilometer mixed-terrain loop that connects Spanish Catalunya with the southeast of France. The route starts and finishes in Girona and is about half paved, using gravel roads, greenways, and quiet backroads to string it all together.

### Geographic Zones Crossed

1. **Girona (Start/Finish)** - Medieval city, cycling capital of Europe
2. **Via Verde del Carrilet** - 55km smooth gravel greenway (repurposed railway)
3. **La Garrotxa Volcanic Zone** - Natural park with 40 volcanic cones
4. **Olot** - Gateway to Pyrenees foothills
5. **Pyrenean Climbs** - Coll de Coubet, Col d'Ares (1,513m)
6. **French Vallespir** - Cross-border into Arles-sur-Tech, Prats de Mollo
7. **Col de Panissars** - Roman Via Augusta/Via Domitia crossing
8. **Alt Emporda Plain** - Vineyards, DO Emporda wine region
9. **Medieval Villages** - Peralada, Castellod'Empuries, Peratallada, Pals
10. **Costa Brava Coast** - L'Escala, Palamos, Sant Feliu de Guixols
11. **Baix Emporda** - Flat agricultural greenways back to Girona

### Key Towns/Checkpoints (Typical Route)
Girona -> Olot -> Sant Joan de les Abadesses -> Camprodon -> Col d'Ares -> Prats de Mollo -> Arles-sur-Tech -> Le Boulou -> La Jonquera -> Capmany -> Peralada -> Castellod'Empuries -> L'Escala -> Palamos -> Sant Feliu de Guixols -> Girona

### Surface Types

| Surface Type | Approximate % | Description |
|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| Gravel/Dirt Tracks | 55% | Forest tracks, agricultural roads, greenways |
| Asphalt (Secondary Roads) | 45% | Quiet backroads, some urban sections |

### Surface Details by Section

- **Via Verde (Greenway):** Smooth compacted sand/gravel, custom-built bike path
- **Olot to Sant Joan:** Mainly concrete agricultural roads, steep inclines
- **Border Section (Spain-France):** Technical gravel, "road quite simply disappears" - requires 40mm+ tires
- **French Descent:** Rocky, rough descents
- **Emporda Plain:** Agricultural roads, some asphalt paving
- **Coastal Section:** Greenways segregated from traffic (except Sant Antoni de Calonge to Sant Feliu de Guixols)

### Key Climbs

#### 1. Coll de Coubet (from Olot)
- Distance: ~9 km steady climb
- First major climb after Olot
- Reveals fantastic Pyrenees views at summit
- Part of hilliest 70km section with 900m elevation

#### 2. Col d'Ares (Border Crossing) - THE CRUX
**From Camprodon (Spanish side - race direction):**
- Distance: 17.9 km
- Elevation Gain: 612-621m (931m to 1,511m)
- Average Gradient: 3.4%
- Difficulty: Category 3 climb
- Summit: 1,511-1,513m (highest point on route)
- Estimated Time: 1h 38min at 11 km/h pace

**From Arles-sur-Tech (French side - more challenging):**
- Distance: 32 km
- Elevation Gain: 1,250m
- Significantly harder than Spanish approach

### The Hardest Sections (Black Pill Preview)

1. **Olot to Sant Joan de les Abadesses**
   - 70km with 900m elevation
   - Possibly most beautiful but most demanding
   - Two hard hills, Coll de Coubet gives "no respite"

2. **Col d'Ares**
   - "Really hard and a huge nightmare for a lot of participants"
   - Many riders DNF at this point
   - Mental milestone: "if they achieved it, they knew they could finish"

3. **Technical Border Descent**
   - After climbing to border, road disappears
   - "Fairly technical, awesome stretch of gravel"
   - Requires 40mm+ tires minimum
   - "Anyone on tyres under 40c would be on foot"
   - "Some of the lumpier gravel descents were impassable on anything but a front-suspension MTB"

### Scenic Highlights

- **La Garrotxa Volcanic Zone:** 40 volcanic cones, beech forests, La Fageda d'en JordÃ 
- **Pyrenean Border Views:** Breathtaking panoramas from Col d'Ares
- **Medieval Villages:** Peratallada (feudal appearance), Pals (archaeological museum), Palau-sator (ancient walls)

### Racing Conditions Analysis

**Positives:**
- September is one of the **best months for cycling** in Girona
- Comfortable temperatures (not summer heat)
- Good sunshine probability
- Bike rental fleets "completely sold out" in September (peak cycling season)

**Challenges:**
- 24% daily rain chance - pack rain gear
- Late September temperatures drop toward evening
- Night riding portion (after sundown ~9:23 PM) will be cool
- Col d'Ares summit at 1,500m significantly cooler than valley

### Pyrenees-Specific Weather

- Climate variable by altitude and orientation
- Spanish (south-facing) side in rain shadow = drier, sunnier
- Thunderstorms can develop late in day during summer
- No "typical" weather pattern in shoulder seasons
- Winds generally blow from humid Atlantic north

### Clothing Recommendations

**Daytime:**
- Lightweight, breathable cycling kit
- Sun protection (sunglasses, sunscreen, arm sleeves)
- Light rain jacket (packable)

**Evening/Night Portion:**
- Arm/leg warmers or thermal layer
- Full finger gloves
- Clear/yellow lenses for night riding
- Waterproof layer for potential rain

**Summit (Col d'Ares):**
- Extra layer for descent
- Wind protection essential

## 5. FORUM INSIGHTS

### Bike Selection Debates

**The Consensus:**
> "All bikes are allowed, although probably the most appropriate are gravel or cyclo-cross bikes."

**The Reality:**
- Via Verde sections: Smooth enough for road bikes
- Col d'Ares: Road climb - "painfully slow on a mountain bike"
- Technical border sections: "Impassable on anything but front-suspension MTB" for some descents

**Recommended Builds:**
- Canyon Grail, Specialized Diverge mentioned as ideal
- 40mm+ tires essential for technical sections
- "Anyone on tyres under 40c would be on foot"
- Road bikes with 28mm tires allowed but challenging
- MTB 29er with slicks works but slow on climbs

### Wild Camping Intel

> "There's no lack of camping sites in this corner of Spain, and Catalonia is one of the few regions in Spain where wild camping is not prohibited."

This is relevant for multi-day bikepacking attempts rather than race format.

### Coastal Warning

> "Stay clear of the coast in summer due to mass tourism."

September race timing helps avoid peak tourist crowds.

### Race Experience Reports

**Night Riding Reality:**
> "The final 49 km were done during the night, riding really fast through single-track toward Girona. Riders were tired but happy and excited to be close to completing the challenge."

**Sleep Deprivation:**
> "Participants could only sleep 4 hours the night before the race."

**Race Start Dynamics:**
> "There was also a half race modality with 145 km, and both started together, making the first phase of the race very fast if riders wanted to stay at the front of the peloton. After the first checkpoint, some riders chose to be more conservative and save energy for the harder parts."

**The Col d'Ares Mental Game:**
> "Col d'Ares was their main goal in their minds - if they achieved it, they knew they could finish the race on time."

### Pacing Strategy

The "ultimate challenge" within the challenge:
- Start: 6:13 AM
- Sundown: ~9:23 PM
- **Target: Finish in 15h 10m to avoid ANY night riding**
- Standard target: Finish before midnight (18h time limit)

Most riders will ride the final 2-3 hours in darkness.

## 6. BLACK PILL - Honest Truths About This Ultra-Distance Challenge

### The Distance Reality
340 km in a single push. This is not a "long ride" - it's an ultra-endurance event requiring months of specific preparation.

### The Elevation Truth
3,000-3,400m of climbing. The race profile isn't about one big climb - it's relentless undulation with two significant mountain passes.

### The Technical Sections
- **Border crossing descent:** "Road quite simply disappears"
- Gravel so rough that riders under 40mm tires will walk
- Some sections genuinely require MTB handling skills
- The race description undersells the technical difficulty

### The Col d'Ares Crucible
> "It was really hard and a huge nightmare for a lot of participants, and some of them left the race on that point."

This climb has historically been the DNF point. Coming at roughly the halfway mark, after already significant climbing, it breaks riders mentally and physically.

### The Time Pressure
- 18-hour limit sounds generous for 340km
- Reality: 55% gravel, 3,400m climbing, technical sections
- Average speed needed: ~19 km/h INCLUDING all stops
- The "ultimate challenge" (daylight finish): requires ~22+ km/h average
- Night riding is not optional for most finishers

### The Sleep Deprivation Factor
- Race starts at dawn (~6:13 AM)
- Festival environment night before = poor sleep
- 12-18 hours of physical exertion
- Decision-making deteriorates after hour 12-14
- Navigation errors increase in darkness

### The September Weather Gamble
- 24% daily rain chance
- Mountain weather unpredictable
- Cold, wet descent from Col d'Ares in rain = hypothermia risk
- Finishing in darkness adds cold exposure

### The Self-Support Reality
- Checkpoint-based, not fully supported
- Must carry enough nutrition between aid stations
- Mechanical issues = your problem
- Navigation errors = your time loss

### The Record Perspective
- Course record: 12 hours
- That's an average of 28.3 km/h INCLUDING gravel, climbing, technical sections
- Elite ultra-endurance athletes only
- Mere mortals should plan for 14-17 hours

### Who Should NOT Do This Race
- First-time ultra-distance riders (do something shorter first)
- Riders without night riding experience
- Those uncomfortable on rough gravel/technical terrain
- Anyone unable to complete 250km+ training rides
- Riders without proper bike setup (40mm+ tires, reliable lights, navigation)

### Who This Race IS For
- Experienced ultra-endurance cyclists
- Gravel riders seeking a landmark European challenge
- Cyclists with strong climbing legs
- Those who thrive on type-2 fun
- Riders who want a prestigious finisher badge

## 7. LOGISTICS SNAPSHOT

### Nearest Airports

#### Girona-Costa Brava Airport (GRO)
- **Distance to Girona:** 12-15 km
- **Pros:** Closest, easiest logistics
- **Cons:** Limited flights (mostly European budget carriers), reduced winter schedule
- **Transport Options:**
  - Sagales bus to Girona center: ~2.50 EUR (bike box allowed)
  - Taxi: 20-25 EUR
  - Pre-booked transfer: 50 EUR (Girona Cycling)

#### Barcelona El Prat Airport (BCN)
- **Distance to Girona:** 118 km
- **Pros:** Major international hub, more flight options, often cheaper
- **Cons:** Requires onward transport to Girona
- **Transport Options:**
  - Sagales bus direct to Girona: ~20 EUR (bike box OK, no changes)
  - Train: 12-25 EUR (complex with bike - must use slow "Rodalies" service)
  - Private taxi/transfer: 210-260 EUR (1.5 hours)
  - **Best for bikes:** Private transfer or bus

### Train Notes (Important!)
- AVE high-speed trains: **NO bike boxes allowed**
- Rodalies slow trains: Bike boxes OK but multiple changes
- Barcelona Sants to Girona: Regional train every hour, <10 EUR, 75-90 mins
- Recommendation: Bus or private transfer with bike

### Girona Bike Services

- **Velodrom.cc:** Workshop, services, airport transfers adapted for cyclists
- **Easy Drive Girona:** Vans equipped for bike transport, group shuttles
- **Eat Sleep Cycle:** Full-service cycling hub, rentals, tours
- **Girona Cycling:** Transfers, guided rides, local expertise

### Accommodation

**Race Weekend:**
- Sea Otter Europe brings 68,000+ visitors
- **Book early** - accommodation sells out
- Pavellon Fontajau area convenient for start

**Recommended:**
- Hotel Carlemany Girona: "Bike Friendly" certified, secure bike storage
- Hotels in Costa Brava (Calella de Palafrugell) if wanting beach base
- Girona old town: Walk to start, atmosphere, restaurants

**Budget Options:**
- Not readily available race weekend
- Consider staying slightly outside city
- Wild camping legal in Catalonia (for pre/post race adventures)

### Timing Logistics

**Typical Race Weekend Schedule:**
- Friday: Accreditation pickup, bike prep, expo
- Saturday: RACE DAY (start ~6:13 AM)
- Sunday: Sea Otter Europe festival, recovery

### Essential Gear Checklist

**Navigation:**
- GPS computer with GPX loaded
- Backup phone mount + offline maps
- Route book (provided at registration)

**Lighting:**
- Powerful front light (1000+ lumens recommended)
- Rear light
- Spare batteries/power bank

**Clothing:**
- Full kit for temperature range 10-26C
- Rain jacket (packable)
- Clear/yellow glasses for night
- Arm/leg warmers

**Bike Setup:**
- 40mm+ tires minimum (42-45mm ideal)
- Tubeless with sealant + plug kit
- Multi-tool, spare tube, pump
- Chain lube

**Nutrition:**
- Capacity for 4+ hours between checkpoints
- Caffeinated options for night section
- Electrolytes (September can be warm)
