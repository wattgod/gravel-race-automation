## RACE HISTORY

### Origins
- **Inaugural Event:** October 2-3, 2021
- Coincided with Trinidad's 200th year Santa Fe Trail celebration
- Life Time searched for a community with untapped off-road potential
- Trinidad selected for its gravel network, history, emerging arts scene, and outdoor recreation initiatives

### Why Trinidad?
- Town leaders established Office of Outdoor Recreation to transform Trinidad into outdoor mecca
- 1,600+ miles of gravel roads in Las Animas County
- Largest county in Colorado (4,775 square miles)
- Remote, undiscovered terrain unlike typical Colorado destinations
- Old West character and history

## TERRAIN & COURSE

### Surface Breakdown
- **90%+ maintained gravel roads**
- Mostly rolling terrain (different from steep Colorado mountain passes)
- Hard-packed, well-maintained surfaces
- Minimal washboarding (notable exception to typical gravel road conditions)
- Some described it as "champagne gravel" and "buttery smooth"

### Technical Sections
- 2-mile technical gravel stretch on South Shore Trail (Trinidad Lake State Park)
- Some sections of two-track
- Burro Canyon: well-packed gravel with mild 9-mile climb (~2% average grade)
- Short sections of blacktop/asphalt, including a "brutal climb on blacktop" near finish

### Landscape & Scenery
- **Spanish Peaks backdrop** ("huajatolla" - Comanche for "double mountain")
- Expansive mesas
- High desert feel with parched earth
- Views of snowcapped peaks
- Old railroads, dilapidated brick homes, Americana ruins
- Transitions from wide-open plains to arid foothills to aspen/pine forests near 9,000 ft
- Fisher's Peak views
- Ranching and mining history visible throughout

### Course Characteristics by Distance

**Stubborn Delores (110mi):**
- Starts just north of New Mexico border at 6,025 ft elevation
- Tops out at nearly 9,000 ft
- 31 miles between aid stations on backside
- Limited to no cell service on remote sections
- "The elevation and climbing will humble you"

**Anteloop (70mi):**
- Desert terrain mixed with snowcapped mountain views
- Gradual climbs
- "Champagne gravel" surface

**Frijole (40mi):**
- Smooth gravel paths
- Breathtaking scenic views
- Manageable climbs
- Good for first-timers

### Tire Recommendations
- 35-40mm tires recommended despite fast, smooth sections
- Moots Routt RSL (race bike recommendation) fits up to 45mm
- Tubeless setup recommended
- 28-32 PSI suggested for 42c tires

## BLACK PILL (Brutal Truths)

### The Hard Realities

**1. Altitude Will Humble You**
- Start line sits at 6,025 ft elevation
- Course tops out near 9,000 ft
- "The elevation and climbing will humble you, especially on the 110mi"
- Sea-level athletes face significant performance degradation
- Altitude sickness possible above 8,000 ft (headache, fatigue, dizziness)

**2. Cutoffs Are Unforgiving**
- Spanish Peaks Inn cutoff (Mile 42.8): 1:20 PM - miss it and you're done
- Trinidad Lake State Park cutoff (Mile 106): 7:40 PM
- One rider report: "pulled from race because I missed second cutoff by just a few minutes... absolutely devastated, a blubbering mess"

**3. Remote and Isolated**
- 31 miles between aid stations on 110mi backside
- No cell service on significant portions
- "There is no shame in turning back" - organizer guidance
- If you have a mechanical, you're potentially stranded

**4. Deceptive "Rolling" Terrain**
- Marketed as "mostly rolling" but 10,613 ft of climbing on 110mi is brutal
- Cumulative effect at altitude is punishing
- Wind late in race can turn return sections into sufferfests

**5. Hydration Crisis Potential**
- High desert environment is dehydrating
- Aid station spacing requires serious bottle capacity
- Multiple reports of running dry between stations

**6. Small Town Logistics**
- Trinidad is remote (200 miles from Denver, 130 from Colorado Springs)
- Limited lodging options - book early or face long drives
- Small town infrastructure strained by 1,000+ participants

**7. Weather Roulette**
- Late September can bring rain, wind, or early cold snaps
- One participant noted "weather was looking questionable"
- No shelter on exposed mesa sections

**8. Underprepared Participants Common**
- Multiple reports of being "heavily underprepared"
- Course demands more than casual gravel riders expect
- Not a beginner-friendly 110-miler

**9. DNF Shame Spiral**
- One rider "rode for another 32 miles after bonking so hard could barely steady myself"
- Emotional investment in finishing can lead to poor decisions
- Long lonely ride back if you drop

**10. Life Time Event Costs**
- Life Time events tend toward premium pricing
- Travel + lodging + entry adds up quickly for a race in a remote location

### Who Should Race This

**Ideal For:**
- Experienced gravel racers seeking new terrain
- Altitude-acclimated or Colorado-based riders
- Those seeking UNBOUND qualifier with better odds
- Riders who value quality gravel over technical challenge
- Community-oriented participants who enjoy festival atmosphere

**Think Twice If:**
- Sea-level athlete with no altitude prep time
- Uncomfortable with remote, unsupported sections
- Need extensive bike shop/mechanical support access
- Prefer large city amenities and lodging options
- First 100-miler (try 70 or 40 first)

### Final Verdict

The Rad Dirt Fest offers some of the finest gravel surface quality in North American racing, set against the dramatic Spanish Peaks backdrop. It's a legitimate endurance test at altitude without the death-defying technical sections of some Colorado events. The Life Time production quality is evident, and the Trinidad community has embraced the event. The remote location and altitude are double-edged: they create the unique character but also demand serious preparation. Not a first gravel race, but an excellent second or third major event for riders building toward UNBOUND or other Life Time Grand Prix races.
