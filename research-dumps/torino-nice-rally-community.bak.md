## Equipment & Gear Recommendations

**Bikes and tire choices:**
"Our stead of choice for this event was All City's do it all beast the Space Horse Disc... paired up with Surly's 41mm All Road Knards! The Knards proved bang up for the job with plenty grip at low pressures on the loose stuff and smooth fast rolling at higher pressures on tarmac. Also can't complaint at 0 punctures between us!" — Team BikeULike blog

"I brung was a Shand Bahookie drop bar with 29×2.2 Pirelli Scorpion XC RC tyres on 9th Wave Cycling Carbon Yarrow Wheels. Designed as a hardpack tyre with a close central tread, ideal for the smooth km, but with pronounced outside knobs for cornering grip when things got more rowdy." — Shand Cycles blog

"The Mason Bokeh with 650B Panaracer tires in 50 mm. In the end, that turned out to be a pretty perfect choice... For 2018 I decided for the other extreme in the range of possibilities: A Gravelbike... In the end, that turned out to be a pretty perfect choice." — Robert Krügel, Mason Cycles (comparing to his 29" rigid MTB with 3-inch tires from the previous year)

"We were all running 1x setups ranging from 42×11-40 to 38×11-42. However, despite this working fine most of the time a lower gear is definitely on the cards for next year! After all it's a ride not a walk." — Team BikeULike blog

**Gearing specifics:**
"He uses the factory-spec Panaracer Comet 27.5 x 50mm tyres, and has modified the gearing by speccing a 34T chainring to claw his way up the steepest mountain passes." — Mason Cycles on Robert Krügel's setup

**Frame bags:**
"The last minute decision to take a couple of buds from Miss Grape was definitely one of my better pre ride decisions." — Team BikeULike blog

"In case you are wondering Tailfin frame bags are waterproof as long as the zip is shut… if its left open they are also excellent at collecting water, I found my passport and money floating in my bag." — Josh Ibbett, joshibbett.substack.com

**Essential clothing:**
"Bring good waterproofs (preferably with a hood), warm waterproof spare gloves and insulated layers that work when wet - Primaloft gilets or climber's belay jackets for example. Waterproof shorts too, especially if your bike doesn't have fenders." — Official TNR FAQs, torino-nice.weebly.com

"It rained on us on the way up [Colle del Colombardo]" — JKB's Bike Ride, 2021

## Terrain Details (Rider Perspective)

**Colle del Colombardo (Day 1):**
"The wall like lower slopes are paved, however I found myself pinned in first gear grovelling up the road as my legs accustomed themselves to pedalling uphill. As the gradient lowered the surface deteriorated to a rough rocky dirt track making for slow going and much sweating. Don't underestimate the Colle Del Colombardo, its tough and slow going." — Josh Ibbett, joshibbett.substack.com

"Once we hit the 'gravel' aka a 10km rock garden we were off the bike and pushing for large sections." — Team BikeULike blog

"It was steep in places, loose and went on for long enough to put a bit of fear into everyone about what the days ahead would hold." — Claire Sharpe, All Terre Adventures

**Colle delle Finestre:**
"The first 12km are winding singletrack road, at an easier gradient than the Colombardo, to a fountain outside an closed up chalet which is also the start of the gravel of the Strada dell'Assietta... The day was heating up as I rode the final 8km of gravel to the summit." — Shand Cycles blog

"It was a long gravel climb that probably would have been empty had it not been for a huge rally... The rally was made up of cars that were at least 20 years old, and there were a lot of them." — Josh Ibbett blog

**Strada dell'Assietta:**
"A 34km long dirt road running along an alpine ridgeway almost entirely above 2,000m up and has been heralded as one of the most spectaculars roads in the world!" — Team BikeULike blog

"Be a little mindful around the blind bends as the route can be busy with motorbikes." — Shand Cycles blog

"The Strada Dell Assiette seemed to be a haven for these heavily laden lumbering beasts [motorbikes] that threw up great clouds of dust and at times squeezed me into the rougher rocks at the edge of the track." — Josh Ibbett, BIKEPACKING.com

**Col du Peas (alternate route):**
"The hike-a-bike starts not far from the refuge. If you've ridden the col d'Izoard previously and up for something different, this section is a piece of remoteness away from ambient noise or mechanical access into the hills... The crux of the hike is around 2.5km in, steep and loose hiking trail that requires some deft manoeuvrability of both bike and feet. It's brief at least." — Shand Cycles blog

"The climb took us four hours, we push our bikes for most of it. We are overtaken by hikers armed with trekking poles, who also struggle to haul themselves up this steep, rocky hill." — Café du Cycliste blog (Day 4)

**"Little Peru" / Strada Della Gardetta:**
"This plateau of alpine pasture, almost free of human activity, undulates between 2300 et 2500m high. It's mineral, unstable and it reveals itself bit by bit as we go along a demanding trail, but one without any technical difficulties. It's not just the beauty. The majesty of this place really makes our heads spin." — Café du Cycliste blog

"For those unversed in ways of the refugio... it's an alpine hut that offers somewhere to stay, food and drink and a packed lunch for the next day if you want it." — JKB's Bike Ride blog

**Via del Sale:**
"The ascent was boring, but the dirt road of Via del Sale was truly sensational. Up there with the best roads I have ever cycled." — JKB's Bike Ride blog

**Death Road descent:**
"Emerging from thick fog at the summit of Col Sampeyre only to descend the 'Death Road', an abandoned road down a sheer sided valley, as night fell. This was like a scene from a zombie apocalypse film, where one wrong move would have you off the edge and hurtling to the rocky river below! We had been warned to use our lights in the tunnels as rockfalls were common and as the road was no longer is use no one would clear them." — Team BikeULike blog

**Sketchy shortcut:**
"I just checked the map and saw a nice direct route down to the valley below. In hindsight there is probably a reason that the Torino Nice organisers don't purposely send riders down this long lost road through a deep gorge... by the time I found the massive rock slide that had wiped the road away I had descended a good 1000m of elevation. I was faced with the option of retracing my steps, or the considerably more sketchy option of unloading my bike and shuttling it and luggage across the scattered rocks. I chose the latter and it made for an adrenaline filled half an hour!" — Josh Ibbett blog

## Race Strategy & Pacing

**Daily approach:**
"We'd given ourselves 8 days to ride the TNR, which meant averaging around 85km and a little over 2,000m climbing every day. I'd done my best to match that with the daily distances in Greece, so I was pretty confident I could do that without too much difficulty in around 6-7 hours pedalling a day." — JKB's Bike Ride blog

"We settled into a bit of a routine. Get up, ride up a mountain (or three) and then find somewhere to stay for the night. We stopped measuring the kilometres ridden each day in favour of checking the vertical metres gained." — JKB's Bike Ride blog

"Almost every day, Strava would inform me I'd done my biggest climb to date." — JKB's Bike Ride blog

**Timing strategy:**
"A couple of German TNR riders also staying at the campsite. We'd bumped into one of them on the pass earlier in the day – it was nice to have some company in the form of other riders... We bumped into a few more TNR riders the following morning (having left at a more sensible time ourselves)." — JKB's Bike Ride blog (suggesting later starts work well)

"I awoke at dawn on day 2 with the Colle Delle Finestre looming overhead... starting early made the experience much more pleasant after the sweltering temperatures of the previous afternoon." — Josh Ibbett blog

**Resupply points:**
"We resupplied at a supermarket in San Antonio (the first of many pavement picnics) before pushing on to Bussoleno for a Maxibon." — Claire Sharpe, All Terre Adventures

"After a road climb over the border to France I took refuge in the traditional French haven for bikepackers… McDonlads. Calories, aircon, charging and use of a toilet, what more could a smelly dirtbag want to refuel and cool down?" — Josh Ibbett, BIKEPACKING.com

**Accommodation flexibility:**
"All this meant a lot of hours spent riding and sitting around in kit. Chammy time became the next metric we started tracking after realising we still had 'straps up' while sitting in a little restaurant waiting for dinner at 10pm." — JKB's Bike Ride blog

"This varied approach [hotels, refugios, and bivvying] felt good. We were free to decide each day how far we wanted to ride, when to stop and were able to follow the flow of the day without having to put our heads down to get somewhere because of a booking." — JKB's Bike Ride blog

**Route choices:**
"We bumped into some Croatian TNR riders at a cafe, who had left Turin the previous morning – cycling almost twice as fast as us. They were planning on finishing in 4-5 days, which is quite impressive. In contrast, we'd met some other cyclists the previous day who had no time-frame at all." — JKB's Bike Ride blog

## DNF Risk Factors

**Mechanical issues:**
"Besides some sleet on the day we climbed over our highest pass (Col Agnel at 2,700m), everything else went smoothly, except for Wes' brakes. After the long, wet descent, his front and rear pistons needed regular manual encouragement to retract properly. Unable to get new pads (he had packed the wrong spares) and get a proper service meant that for John and I the possibility of Wes's bike making it to Nice became our top topic of conversation." — Flow Mountain Bike blog

**Missing parts:**
"After a tour of all the bike shops in town on the hunt for a replacement mech hanger (note to self, take a spare next time!!)" — Team BikeULike blog

**Storm damage complications:**
"The region seems to be under a veil, waiting. Its wounds are open, we can feel them. We have to turn back during our descent to Breil-sur-Roya, which forces us to take the train towards the col of Turini, the true doorway to the Nice region, via the historic road of la Maglia." — Café du Cycliste blog (Storm Alex damage in 2020)

**Physical demands:**
"By this time in the day I have to admit I was feeling it. A day of altitude and 30degree temperatures had me feeling drained." — Josh Ibbett, BIKEPACKING.com

**Mosquitos:**
"We'd already scoped out a bivvy spot for that evening just 1km out of the town which came with an amazing view…and hoards of Asian Tiger Mosquitos. Mosquitos like to bite me, a lot, so I also had the pleasure of riding the rest of the rally with 42, itchy af, mosquito bites." — Claire Sharpe, All Terre Adventures
