# TOUR DIVIDE — COMMUNITY RESEARCH

## Rider Quotes & Race Reports

**Robin Gemperle (2025 Winner, 11d:19h:14m):**
- "Honestly, it was a truly smooth ride. I've been well prepared, knew what was coming next at basically every relevant turn and witnessed pretty much the country I was expecting... I don't mean I was bored though; managing your bike, body and resources throughout this terrain is enough of a challenge even if everything goes according to plan." (pedaled.com)
- "I have not really compared a lot of data so far but i think my time standing still is pretty much within normal ranges. What's probably less normal is the amount of breaks this time is made up of. Usually there were two real breaks per day: One for the night and one to properly refuel. When sleeping I usually slept for at least 4 hours, often 5 and made sure I used the time around it to look after myself and eat just about as much as the bear I had met." (pedaled.com)
- "Average speed as a concrete number is not really something I work with in advance. But as I was aware of my shape, I felt like making use of this by not really limiting myself a lot in terms of power numbers or heart rate and just go hard from the gun." (pedaled.com)

**Edyn Teitge (2024, youngest solo rider at age 15):**
- "One of the most memorable things from day one, and I can't say it is a very fond memory, was Koko Claims. This steep road is impossible to ride because of the big rocks that even make it difficult to walk up. And when we reached the top the downhill wasn't much better, there were a ton of stream crossings and big snow patches which forced us to walk almost as much as we did on the uphill." (rodeo-labs.com)
- "On day one I rolled into Fernie around midnight and was able to get a room at a hostel after a little resupply and dinner at the 24-hour 7 11." (rodeo-labs.com)
- "Day two is when the nice weather came to an end... Up at the summit of the second pass of the day is where I met the first bit of snow. It was coming down in big flakes but luckily it wasn't quite cold enough for the snow to start sticking to the ground." (rodeo-labs.com)

**Jochen Böhringer (2025, 3rd place):**
- "My only real issue right now is some serious saddle soreness that gets particularly bad in the evenings, though it eases overnight. To cope, I will continue to stop at lodges to shower and wash my cycling shorts overnight whenever possible." (ultra-jo.com)
- "One unforgettable highlight was spotting a mountain lion right on the trail in front of me when I was close to Yellow Stone national park. Initially, I thought it was a deer grazing, but when it ran, I realized it was a big cat. Luckily, it was just as startled by me as I was by it, and it ran off safely." (ultra-jo.com)
- "The following day brought me through the brutal heat of the Great Basin, with temperatures soaring to 34°C and no shade to offer relief." (ultra-jo.com)

**Shannon Allison (2025, left at Breckinridge after 1700+ miles):**
- "What I learned about myself: I learned I have zero desire to actually race anymore. What I want most in my life is adventure and new roads." (ohiogravelgrinders.com)
- "Gear mic drop: The bungee webbing that I bought to go over my rear rack was the best piece of gear. I would stuff rain gear/wind breaker in it for quick changes, I've stuffed several burritos and pizza boxes under it and extra bottles of electrolytes!" (ohiogravelgrinders.com)
- "I think the friendships that I forged throughout the tour took me off guard. You're not supposed to have emotional support as a racer but I chose to make friends and leave behind the notion of racing." (ohiogravelgrinders.com)

**Ty Domin (2017 rider):**
- "The magic of my experience was further amplified by the sense of changing seasons as I gradually travelled toward the equator, despite just over 19 days elapsing. (bikepacking.com)
- "I'll go further and assert that the effort of climbing a mountain or crossing a continent serves only to enhance its delights and majesty in a manner no leisurely wanderings can approach." (bikepacking.com)

**Mike Hall (historical perspective, before his death in 2017):**

## Terrain Details (Rider Perspective)

**Koko Claims (Early in Canadian Rockies):**
- "This steep road is impossible to ride because of the big rocks that even make it difficult to walk up. And when we reached the top the downhill wasn't much better, there were a ton of stream crossings and big snow patches which forced us to walk almost as much as we did on the uphill." — Edyn Teitge (rodeo-labs.com)

**"The Wall" (Northern section):**
- "I had heard rumors of 'The Wall' but wasn't quite sure what it was, it turns out the name is pretty accurate. It is a very narrow trail that is only a few miles long but it certainly is not an easy few miles. The crux or the actual wall is less than half a mile long but it is very steep with lots of downed trees crossing the trail and to add on top of it all the constant rain all day made it a mudhole." — Edyn Teitge (rodeo-labs.com)

**Fleecer Ridge (Montana):**
- "Hike-a-bike at Fleecer Ridge. Passed Wise River, Montana. Ended up at some random campsite after not quite reaching Lima, Montana." — Referenced in halfwayanywhere.com data table

**Great Divide Basin (Wyoming):**
- "The following day brought me through the brutal heat of the Great Basin, with temperatures soaring to 34°C and no shade to offer relief. The gravel roads continued" — Jochen Böhringer (ultra-jo.com)
- "The pass before Lima was one of the most brutal, it wasn't super steep or technical but it had been re-graded with chunky gravel that made the riding agonizingly slow and the fact it was almost 100 degrees certainly didn't help." — Edyn Teitge (rodeo-labs.com)

**Togwotee Pass (Grand Teton area):**
- "I climbed up Togwotee pass in the breathtaking Grand Teton national park at around 3,000 meters elevation... The scenery today was truly stunning—amazing views of snow-capped peaks, beautiful lakes, and quiet valleys, with impressive flora including vibrant flowers amidst lush greenery." — Jochen Böhringer (ultra-jo.com)

**Atlantic City, Wyoming:**
- "Passed Atlantic City, Wyoming (terrible place)" — From halfwayanywhere.com data table

**Final stretch to border:**
- "The final 120-mile stretch is predominantly pavement. After an anxious pass through Silver City, the route's last 50 miles of dirt are smooth, sandy rollers across the upper Chihuahuan desert. Remaining time seems to decelerate as the visual monotony of Yucca and Mesquite--broken only by darting Jackrabbits--sweeps incessantly into Mexico." (tourdivide.blogspot.com)

## Race Strategy & Pacing

**Robin Gemperle's winning strategy (2025):**
- "I got excited about increasing my expectations throughout the race: at first it was about winning, then the record, then Lachy inofficial time and after 6 days, a finishing time above 12 days would have felt disappointing." (pedaled.com)
- "Usually there were two real breaks per day: One for the night and one to properly refuel. When sleeping I usually slept for at least 4 hours, often 5." (pedaled.com)
- "Not necessarily short stopping times, but breaks that allow you to do what has to be done." (pedaled.com)
- "It seems like being a great athlete is just as important nowadays and I really like that." (pedaled.com)

**Pacing data from Halfway Anywhere (19d:17h:18m finish):**
- "I started the ride with a goal finish time of under 25 days, which I revised to under 20 days after the fourth day." (halfwayanywhere.com)
- Average daily distance: 137.44 mi / 221.19 km
- Average time riding per day: 14:49:29
- Average start time: 6:21 AM
- "The weather was perhaps the most significant factor in dictating daily distance." (halfwayanywhere.com)

**Jochen Böhringer's experience:**
- "I got so tired that I had to take a 20-minute power nap by the roadside, wrapped in my down jacket and pants. When I woke, the sunrise had started, and I felt ready to continue, as before that, I couldn't even hold my handlebars straight or keep my eyes open. Surprisingly, four caffeine tablets had no effect on my exhaustion." (ultra-jo.com)
- "I spent almost the entire day relying on gummy bears and whatever water I could find from roadside sources." (ultra-jo.com)

**Edyn Teitge's approach:**
- "I decided to spend the night at a hotel in Eureka to try and get a chance to warm up and dry all my gear and I was lucky enough to have gotten the last room available." (rodeo-labs.com)
- "I called a bike shop and asked if they could set a new tire and a few other things outside because I knew I would probably get there after hours." (rodeo-labs.com)

## DNF Risk Factors

**Saddle sores:**
- "My only real issue right now is some serious saddle soreness that gets particularly bad in the evenings, though it eases overnight. To cope, I will continue to stop at lodges to shower and wash my cycling shorts overnight whenever possible. Other than that, everything is going smoothly. Fingers crossed the soreness improves." — Jochen Böhringer (ultra-jo.com)
- "My saddle sores worsened today, with two main contact points becoming quite raw and painful, especially on rough trails. I cleaned the wounds at a visitor center and applied blister plasters, which helped somewhat, but I'm not optimistic about a quick recovery since the pressure persists." — Jochen Böhringer (ultra-jo.com)

**Mechanical issues:**
- "On the descent of the first pass I hit a rock hard and I immediately heard air rushing out of my tire. I had slashed a hole in my sidewall." — Edyn Teitge (rodeo-labs.com)
- "About ten miles out from Whitefish, my flat repair failed me. So I once again had to throw in another tube." — Edyn Teitge (rodeo-labs.com)

**Dehydration/nutrition:**
- "The only real problem was that everything was still closed early morning—no place to get food anywhere on the route. I even made a short detour to a point of interest hoping for something, but no luck there either. So, I spent almost the entire day relying on gummy bears and whatever water I could find from roadside sources." — Jochen Böhringer (ultra-jo.com)
- "Last evening I descended from Union Pass, which sits close to 3,000 meters elevation, and found a shelter to sleep in for the night. However, the real challenge was a severe lack of water—there was none to be found nearby except stagnant puddles, so I opted to stop rather than risk riding in the dark without reliable water sources." — Jochen Böhringer (ultra-jo.com)

**Sleep deprivation:**
- "I got so tired that I had to take a 20-minute power nap by the roadside... I couldn't even hold my handlebars straight or keep my eyes open." — Jochen Böhringer (ultra-jo.com)

**Weather/mud:**
- "A thunderstorm hit and turned the road to peanut butter mud, which stopped me for a bit while I waited for the mud to dry." — From halfwayanywhere.com data table
