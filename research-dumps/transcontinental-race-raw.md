# OFFICIAL DATA

## Race Overview
- **Full Name**: The Transcontinental Race (TCR)
- **Type**: Self-supported, ultra-distance cycling race
- **Location**: Across Europe (route changes annually)
- **Typical Distance**: 3,200 - 5,000+ km (varies by edition)
- **Typical Elevation**: 40,000 - 64,000+ meters climbing
- **Time Limit**: Approximately 15-16 days
- **Format**: Single-stage (clock never stops from start to finish)
- **Organizer**: Lost Dot (since 2017)
- **Website**: https://www.lostdot.cc/tcr

## Key Rules (10 Rules / Spirit of the Race)
1. **Self-Supported**: No third-party support, resupply, or lodgings arranged in advance
2. **No Drafting**: Riding close together with other riders is prohibited
3. **Solo Navigation**: Riders must plan and navigate their own route
4. **Mandatory Control Points**: Must visit all checkpoints and complete parcours
5. **Commercially Available Services Only**: Can use hotels, restaurants, bike shops open to public
6. **GPS Tracker Required**: Position uploaded every 5 minutes via Follow My Challenge
7. **Brevet Card**: Must be stamped at each control point
8. **No Pre-Arranged Support**: Cannot receive help from friends/family along route
9. **Road-Legal Bike**: Human-powered only
10. **Social Interaction Allowed**: Brief stops with other riders permitted; sustained coordination is not

## Entry Requirements
- **Entry Fee**: Approximately GBP 350 (2022 reference)
- **Application Process**: Online application with deadline (typically December for following year)
- **Capacity**: ~350 riders (selected from 1,000+ applicants)
- **Categories**: Solo riders and pairs
- **Age Requirement**: 18+
- **Medical Certificate**: Required
- **Insurance**: Required

# RACE HISTORY

## Founding
- **Founded**: 2013 by Mike Hall
- **Inspiration**: Mike's 2012 World Cycle Race experience
- **Original Route**: London to Istanbul
- **First Edition**: 30 starters

## Mike Hall (1981-2017)
- British cyclist and race organizer
- Won World Cycle Race 2012 (set record)
- Won Tour Divide 2013 and 2016
- Won Trans Am Bike Race 2014
- **Death**: March 31, 2017 - struck by car during Indian Pacific Wheel Race in Australia
- Had covered 5,024 km of 5,500 km distance
- Memorial service held in Harrogate, May 2, 2017
- TCR No.5 (2017) went ahead in his honor with largest turnout to date

## Lost Dot Organization
- Created after Mike Hall's death by his colleagues
- Administers TCR and other races (The Accursed Race, Lost Dot 101)
- Introduced Mike Hall Bursary for riders from lower-income households
- Runs #100TCRWomen campaign to increase diversity

# TERRAIN & COURSE

## Course Philosophy
- **Free Route Format**: Riders choose their own path between mandatory points
- **Mandatory Control Points**: Typically 4 checkpoints with parcours
- **Parcours**: Mandatory route sections (5-100+ km) often in mountains
- **No Set Route**: Only start, finish, and control points defined

## Typical Terrain Types

### Road Surfaces
- **Predominantly Paved Roads**: Most riders optimize for good tarmac
- **Gravel Sections**: At least one parcours includes unpaved/gravel since 2015
- **Cobblestones**: Western Europe (Belgium, France) - classic cycling territory
- **Variable Quality**: Eastern Europe/Balkans have rougher roads
- **Unrideable Sections**: Occasional walking required on parcours

### Geographic Regions Typically Crossed
1. **Western Europe** (Belgium, France, Switzerland)
   - Well-maintained roads
   - Established cycling infrastructure
   - Higher cost of living

2. **The Alps** (Switzerland, Austria, Italy)
   - Major mountain passes (Stelvio, Splugen, etc.)
   - Cold temperatures even in summer
   - Potential snow at high altitude

3. **The Balkans** (Slovenia, Croatia, Bosnia, Serbia, Albania, Kosovo, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Greece)
   - Rougher roads
   - More remote terrain
   - Extreme heat in summer
   - Limited resupply in places
   - Wild camping more acceptable/legal

4. **Eastern Europe** (Romania, Bulgaria, Turkey)
   - Variable road quality
   - Lower costs
   - Language barriers more common

## Elevation Characteristics
- **Total Climbing**: 40,000 - 64,000+ meters depending on edition
- **Daily Climbing**: Often 1,000+ meters per day
- **Major Passes**: Stelvio (2,757m), Tourmalet, various Alpine cols
- **Mountain Checkpoints**: Control points often at mountain locations

## Notable Terrain Challenges

### The Alps
- Freezing temperatures even in July/August
- Storm conditions on passes
- Snow possible at high altitude
- Long, steep climbs
- Technical descents

### The Balkans
- "Horrible Albanian roads" - worst gravel imaginable
- Old cobblestone roads with half the cobbles missing
- Landmine warnings in Bosnia (marked areas)
- Remote sections with little resupply
- 37,000m+ climbing through Accursed Mountains region

### Road Hazards
- Tunnels (some banned to cyclists)
- Major intersections
- Heavy goods vehicle traffic
- Narrow winding roads
- Unpredictable surface changes

# FORUM INSIGHTS & COMMUNITY WISDOM

## Sleep Strategy Insights

### Elite Approach
- James Mark Hayden (2x winner): ~3 hours per night average
- Christoph Strasser (6x RAAM winner): 6-9 hours TOTAL over 8 days
- Quote: "This amount of sleep was on the absolute limit of what I could sustain while operating at a good level"

### Recommended for First-Timers
- 4-6 hours per night plus occasional naps
- Body adapts to ~2 hours less than normal for 2 weeks
- Quote: "When new to the sport, err on the side of too much sleep"
- Power naps: Even 15 minutes makes huge difference

### Sleep Banking
- Extend sleep 1 hour/night for week before race
- Stay in bed 10+ hours for 3 nights before event
- Proven to improve cognitive function during deprivation

### Cautionary Tale
> "In the first Transcontinental race I did in 2015, I was an idiot and thought sleeping in twenty minute windows like Christof Strasser would be a good idea. It all fell apart horribly."
> - James Hayden (ended up cycling around Ljubljana for an hour in sleep-drunk daze)

## Equipment Recommendations

### Bike Type
- Road bikes still dominant but gravel gaining popularity
- Trend toward wider tires (40-44mm now more common than sub-30mm)
- Gravel frames "quite forgiving" for comfort
- Quote: "32mm road tires were not enough for rough sections"

### Drivetrain
- Shimano Di2 popular (electronic shifting)
- Some prefer mechanical (easier field repair, one less thing to charge)
- Gearing: 50/34 or 46/33 front, 11-32 or 11-36 rear
- Quote: "Snapped gear cables and sore fingers from shifting on mechanical"

### Bags (Typical Setup)
- Saddle pack: 7-9L (Apidura Racing)
- Aerobar pack: 2-2.5L
- Frame bag: 4L
- Top tube bag: 2L

### Lighting
- Son dynamo hubs very popular
- Edelux II front light
- Battery-powered alternatives: Lupine lights
- Critical for night riding (often 70+ hours in single stint for leaders)

### Navigation
- Garmin 1030 primary + backup unit (Wahoo Elemnt Bolt)
- Route loaded on multiple devices
- Backup routes on phone (Komoot, RideWithGPS)

### Sleeping Gear
- Borah Gear bivy or Alpkit Hunka
- Superlight sleeping mat (Klymit Inertia)
- Sea to Summit down quilt
- Quote: "A tent is nice but pitching/packing costs equivalent of 50k riding per day"

### Clothing
- MTB shoes recommended (comfortable for walking, saves space vs separate shoes)
- Merino arm/leg warmers
- Down jacket
- Reflective rain jacket
- Reflective gilet

### Tools & Spares
- Multi-tool
- Mini pump
- Smart tire patches + tire boot
- Rear derailleur hanger
- Spare spokes
- Baby wipes + chamois cream

## Route Planning Wisdom

### Platform Preferences
- **Ride With GPS**: Best for road cycling route building, Google Street View integration
- **Komoot**: Good for surface type info, "bike touring" mode helpful
- **Strava Heatmaps**: Verify roads are actually used by cyclists
- **Google Maps**: Initial route sketching, Street View verification

### Planning Process
1. Enter mandatory parcours/checkpoints into Google Maps
2. Build detailed route in RideWithGPS
3. Check entire route with Strava heatmaps
4. Verify with satellite imagery and Street View
5. Export to Komoot to check surfaces
6. Load on primary and backup devices

### Route Philosophy
> "A flatter, more direct route will generally be busier and faster, but you can still be competitive while avoiding less enjoyable riding conditions."

> "Routing was key. Mine just wasn't good enough, with cobbled forest detours and aborted attempts at Swiss tunnels costing me many hours."

## Training Guidance

### Volume Benchmarks
- Elite level (Kristof Allegaert): 20,000+ km in 7 months before race
- Serious competitor: 15,000+ km
- Typical participant: 5,000-10,000 km in 6 months before
- Monthly breakdown: 500-1,000 km winter, 1,000-1,500 km spring/summer

### Training Philosophy
> "You cannot push extremely hard for 20-30 weeks - you will get bored, burnt out or broken. Build a repeatable week's training you can achieve for 18 out of 20 weeks."

> "Rides longer than about 4-6 hours don't yield additional gains. Very long rides take longer to recover from."

### Physical Demands
- 18-20 hours/day on bike
- Intensity mostly below aerobic threshold (LT1)
- 8,000-10,000 kcal burned per day
- Sleep deprivation
- Night riding
- Lots of climbing

### Experience Over Gadgets
> "Jana Kesenheimer didn't overhaul her training or chase marginal gains. Instead, she leaned on years of consistent volume. For an event that spans an entire continent, that kind of base IS race-specific training."

# BLACK PILL (HARSH REALITIES)

## Finish Rate Statistics
- **Overall Finish Rate**: ~60% (50-75% range depending on year)
- **Finish Within Time Limit for Party**: Only 37% achieve this
- **DNF/Scratch Rate**: ~40-50% each year
- **TCR No.8 (2022)**: 45% scratch rate (highest ever)
- **TCR No.10 (2024)**: 41% scratched (134 of 326 riders)

## When Scratches Happen
- 86% who reach CP3 (two-thirds through) finish the race
- Most scratches occur in first half of race
- Second quarter has highest scratch probability
- ~1% of riders scratch for every 100 km ridden

## Common Reasons for DNF

### Equipment Failures
- Charging system failures (can end race immediately)
- Dodgy power cables preventing GPS charging
- Tracker battery failures
- GPS device crashes
- Quote: "One of the women's favorites scratched on first day when charging system failed"

### Navigation Disasters
- Banned roads/tunnels not checked beforehand
- GPS "route calculation errors" causing wrong turns
- Routing problems: "Cobbled forest detours and aborted attempts at Swiss tunnels costing many hours"

### Physical Collapse
- Quote: "18 hours behind leaders in little over 36 hours... level of mental deflation"
- Accumulated fatigue from first days being too aggressive
- Heat stroke in Balkans
- Hypothermia in Alps

### Mental Breakdown
- Sleep deprivation hallucinations
- Realization of impossibility of time cut
- Loss of motivation
- Quote: "The second quarter of the race being when people are more likely to scratch"

## Sleep Deprivation Effects

### Hallucinations
- Extremely common among participants
- "Feel extremely real and are often very bizarre"
- Examples: conversations with fictitious Alberto Contador, goblin-like creatures jumping from woods
- Quote: "I stopped being able to concentrate... the hallucinations distracted me from the ride"

### Microsleep & Crashes
- Riders falling asleep on bike
- Quote: "What woke me up was an electric fence"
- Quote: "I fell asleep and crashed into fence"

### Cognitive Impairment
- Blurred vision
- Impaired decision making
- Reduced reaction times
- Emotional dysregulation
- Quote: "Days and nights blended into a never-ending continuum of pedaling, eating and occasional short sleep"

### Long-Term Effects
- Mood alterations can last up to a month post-race
- Heightened stress responsivity
- Emotional reactivity

## Safety Risks

### Fatalities
- Mike Hall: Killed during Indian Pacific Wheel Race 2017
- 2017: "Particularly bad year" with three deaths in road-based bikepacking events
- Deaths from vehicle collisions occur across ultra-distance cycling

### Objective Risk Stats
- 10-40 deaths per billion km ridden (Europe, N. America, Australasia)
- 1 cyclist death per 25-100 million km ridden
- Risk accumulates over long distances

### Night Riding Dangers
- Higher fatality rate at night vs daytime
- Dim/unlighted darkness associated with more serious injuries
- Sleep-deprived riding especially dangerous

### Environmental Hazards
- Extreme weather (flooding, heatwaves)
- Remote sections with no cell coverage
- Wildlife
- Poor road surfaces
- Landmines in Bosnia (marked areas)

## Mental Suffering

### The Reality of Suffering
> "My head was trying to force me to go to sleep at all costs... If I was pushing my bike up a hill, I would occasionally find myself stopping for no reason."

> "You just go through the hallucinations and enjoy it. Your brain is so tired, and you're going to see things whether you like it or not."

### Dark Moments
- Extreme loneliness
- Questioning why you're doing this
- Physical pain compounding mental exhaustion
- Quote: "The TCR is not a joke. This is a race where you can get hurt if you are not careful."

### Post-Race Impact
- Research shows "sizable implications for riders' mood"
- Increased fatigue
- Decreased vigor
- Emotional disturbances lasting weeks

# LOGISTICS

## Budget Estimates

### Total Cost Range
- **Minimum Budget**: ~EUR 1,200-1,500
- **Typical Budget**: ~EUR 2,000-3,000
- **Comfortable Budget**: ~EUR 3,500+

### Cost Breakdown

| Category | Minimum | Typical | High |
|----------|---------|---------|------|
| Entry Fee | EUR 300-390 | EUR 350-400 | EUR 400 |
| Travel to/from | EUR 200 | EUR 450 | EUR 1,500 |
| Accommodation | EUR 200 | EUR 600-700 | EUR 900+ |
| Food | EUR 250-350 | EUR 400-500 | EUR 700+ |
| Bike/Gear | Variable | Variable | Variable |
| Medical/Insurance | EUR 50-100 | EUR 100 | EUR 150 |

### Daily Spending
- EUR 50-100 per day on food and accommodation
- Western Europe: ~EUR 20/day food (grocery stores, petrol stations)
- Eastern Europe: ~EUR 15/day food (prices halve in Bosnia+)
- Hotels: EUR 50/night average (can sleep outside to save)

### Money-Saving Tips
- Sleep outside more often (bivy)
- Stock up at grocery stores vs restaurants
- Enter Eastern Europe quickly (lower costs)
- Wild camping legal in Albania, Kosovo, N. Macedonia, Serbia, BiH

## Travel Logistics

### Getting to Start
- Multiple trains often required
- Bike transport fees (EUR 50-200 depending on carrier)
- Early arrival recommended for rest before race

### Getting Home from Finish
- Combination of options: bike riding, ferry, taxi, plane, train
- Can take 1-3 days
- Budget EUR 200-500 for return transport
- Consider: can you ride to a major airport/train hub?

### Visa Requirements
- Check requirements for all countries crossed
- Turkey may require visa (included in some entry fees)
- Schengen zone simplifies much of Europe
- Non-EU countries: Albania, Kosovo, Serbia, Bosnia, Montenegro, N. Macedonia, Turkey

## Accommodation Options

### During Race
- Hotels (most common for serious rest)
- Bivvy on roadside (free but less recovery)
- Bus shelters, benches
- Behind churches
- Petrol stations (24hr services helpful)

### Mixed Strategy
- Most riders use combination
- Hotels when need proper wash/recovery
- Bivvy when time-pressured or remote
- Quote: "1 night hotel before start, 3 nights at finish, 6 nights during race"

## Resupply Strategy

### Food Sources
- Grocery stores
- Petrol stations (often 24hr, hot food)
- Restaurants/cafes
- Bakeries
- Quote: "Purchasing most food at grocery stores and petrol stations"

### Bike Services
- Bike shops in major towns
- Advance research on locations critical
- Some riders carry extensive spares to minimize stops

### Charging
- Hotel rooms
- Cafes/restaurants (buy coffee, ask to charge)
- Some petrol stations
- Dynamo hub (charge while riding)

## Control Point Logistics

### What Happens at Control Points
1. Get brevet card stamped
2. Mandatory rest may be required
3. Complete any parcours requirements
4. Some have specific opening hours

### Control Point Timing
- Plan arrival during opening hours
- Factor in any mandatory waits
- Some have volunteer support, food available

## OVERALL GRAVEL GOD RATING: 9.0/10

### Rating Justification
The Transcontinental Race represents the pinnacle of European ultra-distance cycling. Its free-route format, mandatory self-support rules, and legendary status make it the benchmark against which all other European ultra races are measured. The Mike Hall legacy adds historical significance. Not a pure gravel race (primarily road-focused), but parcours include significant unpaved sections and the Balkans terrain demands gravel-ready equipment and mentality.

## Who Should Consider TCR

### Ideal Candidate Profile
- 2+ years ultra-distance cycling experience
- Completed 1,000+ km multi-day ride
- Comfortable with route planning software
- Can ride 300-400 km days consistently
- Experience with sleep deprivation
- Self-sufficient problem solver
- European geography knowledge helpful

- Complete a 1,200 km+ audax/randonneur

- Night riding experience

# APPENDIX: KEY QUOTES

## On the Race Itself
> "The Transcontinental Race is a single stage race in which the clock never stops. Riders plan, research and navigate their own course and choose when and where to rest. They will take only what they can carry and consume only what they can find."

## On Suffering
> "You just go through the hallucinations and enjoy it. I mean, that's your entertainment. Your brain is so tired, and you're going to see things whether you like it or not."

## On Self-Support
> "No third-party support, resupply or lodgings is allowed. You can only rely on what you are bringing with you and on publicly accessible support like bike repair shops, hotels, and restaurants."

## On Mike Hall's Legacy
> "Mike Hall devised the Transcontinental Race and ran all editions until his tragic death in 2017. His spirit remains the beating heart of everything the organization does."

## On the Challenge
> "The most humbling of all of these results is that despite their best efforts, only about 60% of people who have started the Transcontinental Race have been able to finish."

## On Weather
> "The Transcontinental has really been two races. You have the Alps with the cold temperatures, then the Balkans and Eastern Europe with the heat and the rugged roads."

## On Preparation
> "You cannot push extremely hard for 20-30 weeks - you will get bored, burnt out or broken. The best gains in aerobic fitness and endurance come from consistent work over as long a period as possible."

## On Finishing
> "Both the physical strength - being fit enough to ride for so many hours everyday - and the mental strength - being disciplined and motivated enough to continue every morning - are essential. Neither are easy and finishing these events commands respect."
